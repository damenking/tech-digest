Americans are adopting AI faster, but trusting it less
A new Quinnipiac University poll paints a nuanced picture of where the American public stands on AI: adoption is up, but trust is quietly eroding. Most respondents expressed concern about transparency — they don't know how these systems make decisions — and want stronger regulation. Separately, the same poll found that only 15% of Americans say they'd be willing to have an AI program as their direct supervisor, assigning tasks and setting schedules. That number is surprisingly low given how much enterprise software is already quietly doing exactly that. The trust gap is arguably the most important long-term problem in AI deployment: tools that people use but don't trust tend to be used badly, with users either over-relying on outputs or ignoring them entirely. For developers and product teams, this is a signal that explainability and transparency aren't just nice-to-haves — they're becoming user expectations. The poll didn't break down demographics, but it's a reasonable bet that younger, more tech-adjacent users are more comfortable with AI supervision than the general population. Still, 85% saying 'no thanks' to an AI boss is a number that enterprise AI vendors selling workforce automation tools probably don't want to advertise. The broader societal concern captured in the poll — about AI's impact on jobs, privacy, and accountability — reflects a public that is engaging with AI more critically than the industry sometimes acknowledges. Worth bookmarking as a baseline for how public sentiment is evolving.

About a month ago, the Pentagon made an unusual and aggressive move: it labeled Anthropic a supply chain risk and ordered federal agencies to stop using its AI products. MIT Technology Review characterizes the move as a 'culture war tactic' — essentially an attempt to use national security bureaucracy to punish a company perceived as ideologically misaligned with the current administration. Anthropic, for its part, has been notably vocal about AI safety and has published detailed policy frameworks, which some in the current political environment have read as insufficiently hawkish or too aligned with academic and regulatory caution. The irony is that the Pentagon is one of the largest consumers of AI tools, and cutting off Anthropic would have left agencies scrambling for alternatives in the middle of active deployments. The judge's intervention is a temporary block — a preliminary injunction — not a final ruling, so this fight isn't over. But it signals that courts are willing to scrutinize politically motivated use of national security designations against tech companies. This case matters well beyond Anthropic: if the government can arbitrarily label any AI vendor a 'supply chain risk,' it creates massive uncertainty for the entire enterprise AI market. Every vendor selling to federal agencies now has to think about regulatory risk that has nothing to do with their actual security posture. Watch this one closely.

AI health tools are proliferating — but the evidence for how well they work is thin
Microsoft just launched Copilot Health, letting users connect medical records and ask health questions, and Amazon expanded its Health AI (previously restricted to One Medical members) to a broader audience. MIT Technology Review takes a hard look at whether any of this actually works as advertised, and the honest answer is: it's complicated. Clinical AI tools face a brutal validation challenge — they need to be accurate enough not to cause harm, which requires rigorous testing that most consumer-facing health tools have not yet undergone at scale. The LLM-based tools now flooding the health space are generally good at synthesizing information and explaining concepts, but they struggle with the thing that matters most in medicine: knowing the limits of their own knowledge and escalating appropriately when something is outside their competence. There's also a significant liability grey area — these tools are framed as 'informational' to avoid FDA oversight as medical devices, but users often treat them as diagnostic. For engineers building on top of these platforms, the regulatory landscape is murky: connecting to patient records via APIs creates HIPAA obligations that are non-trivial. For end users, the tools are probably useful for understanding your own test results or preparing questions for a doctor appointment — low-stakes informational use cases. For anything that might affect treatment decisions, independent validation studies are still the thing to look for, and most of the currently launched tools don't have them yet.

Axios npm package compromised — malicious versions dropping a remote access trojan
This is the story every developer needs to see today. Axios, one of the most widely used HTTP client libraries in the JavaScript/Node.js ecosystem, has been compromised on npm, with malicious versions distributing a remote access trojan (RAT). StepSecurity published the disclosure, and it's scoring very high on Hacker News for good reason. Supply chain attacks via npm have become one of the most effective vectors for compromising developer machines and CI/CD pipelines, precisely because developers have a deep habit of installing packages without scrutinizing them. Axios has hundreds of millions of weekly downloads, so even a brief window of malicious versions being live can affect an enormous number of projects. The attack pattern — compromise a popular package and use it to establish persistence on developer machines — is the same playbook used in the event.js and ua-parser-js compromises from previous years. If you or your team use Axios, you should immediately audit your lock files to verify you're not running any of the affected version ranges, and run a security scan on any machine where npm install has been run recently. The LiteLLM/Delve story (also in today's news) is related: LiteLLM was separately hit with credential-stealing malware last week, suggesting this is an active campaign period. Yarn, pnpm, and npm lockfiles all provide some protection, but only if you're actually validating hashes. This is a good moment to review your dependency pinning and integrity verification practices.

This is a significant moment for media regulation: the FCC under the current administration has been broadly permissive on consolidation, and this judicial pushback suggests courts are willing to enforce statutory limits even when the regulator itself is waving deals through. Nexstar is already the largest television station group owner in the U.S.; adding Tegna's stations would have created an unprecedented concentration in local broadcast. For the broader tech and media industry, the case is a signal that consolidation deals structured around regulatory exceptions are legally fragile — especially when those exceptions are granted by an agency that may be overstepping its mandate. The 'immediately cease' language from the judge is unusually strong, suggesting there was ongoing integration work that needed to be stopped urgently. Expect appeals, but this is a meaningful win for those who think broadcast ownership limits should be enforced.
