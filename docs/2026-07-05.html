LLMs are stuck in a groupthink groove — and a startup is trying to fix it
MIT Technology Review ran a sharp piece on a fundamental but underappreciated problem with current LLMs: they exhibit strong statistical biases that make their outputs predictably non-random, even when asked to be random. The classic demonstration is asking any major chatbot for 'a random number between 1 and 10' — you'll almost always get 7, because 7 appears most frequently in training data as a supposedly 'random' choice. This isn't just a parlor trick; it reflects a deeper issue where models converge on the most statistically likely answer rather than genuinely exploring the solution space. A startup is now specifically targeting this groupthink problem, trying to build models or wrappers that can produce more genuinely diverse outputs. The practical implications extend well beyond random number generation — it affects brainstorming, hypothesis generation, creative tasks, and any domain where you want AI to surface non-obvious solutions. The homogeneity problem is also concerning in multi-agent systems where multiple LLM instances are supposed to provide independent perspectives but end up agreeing with each other because they're all drawing from similar distributions. This is one of those problems that's been quietly known in the research community for a while but is now getting more mainstream attention as AI gets deployed in higher-stakes decision-making contexts. The startup's approach isn't fully detailed in the piece, but the problem framing alone is worth understanding if you're building systems that rely on LLM diversity of thought.

NASA's X-59 quiet supersonic demonstrator has been central to generating the scientific data that underpins this regulatory shift. If finalized, this rule change would dramatically expand the commercial viability of supersonic passenger jets, which currently can only fly supersonically over water. It's also worth noting this proposal comes as Boom Supersonic's Overture program has been making development progress — the timing suggests some coordination between industry and regulators. AI's volatile power draw is quietly testing power grid stability limits
IEEE Spectrum has published an important and underreported story about a specific technical challenge AI data centers pose for power grids: it's not just the total energy consumption that's the problem, it's the volatility of that consumption. Traditional data center loads are relatively predictable — servers run at fairly consistent utilization. AI training and inference workloads, by contrast, can swing dramatically in power draw within seconds as GPU clusters spin up and down across jobs. This kind of rapid, large-scale load variation is genuinely new for grid operators, who design for much slower demand fluctuations. The concern is grid instability: frequency deviations, voltage sags, and in extreme cases, cascading failures triggered by the synchronized power swings of multiple large AI facilities. The problem is compounded by the geographic clustering of data centers, which means local grid segments face particularly intense volatility. Some AI facilities are already deploying large battery systems and flywheels specifically to buffer these rapid swings and smooth their grid interface, but this adds cost and complexity. As AI infrastructure continues to scale, grid engineers are going to need to fundamentally rethink how they model and manage large industrial loads. This is one of those infrastructure problems that tends to get little attention until something breaks spectacularly, and the stakes are high given how much critical infrastructure depends on grid stability.
