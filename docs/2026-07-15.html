Microsoft's Secure Boot has been broken for most of a decade — no one noticed until now
Security researchers have revealed that Secure Boot, Microsoft's firmware-level security feature designed to prevent unauthorized operating systems and bootloaders from running during startup, has been bypassable for approximately ten years due to old cryptographic 'shims' that Microsoft never revoked. A shim is essentially a small, signed bootloader intermediary — and if old, compromised shims remain trusted by the Secure Boot chain, attackers can use them to bypass the entire protection. This is a supply chain and key management failure more than a fundamental flaw in the Secure Boot design itself, but the practical impact is the same: a decade of users trusting a security control that was quietly undermined. The attack surface is particularly concerning for enterprise environments and government systems where Secure Boot is often cited as a compliance requirement. Microsoft is now in the position of needing to revoke a large number of old shims, which has to be done carefully to avoid bricking legitimate systems that depend on them. This also echoes the BlackLotus Secure Boot bypass from a few years ago, suggesting that shim management has been a persistent weak point. For sysadmins and security teams, this is a reason to reassess how much trust you place in Secure Boot as a standalone control.

Painted electronic tattoos could be the future of wearable biosensors
Researchers have developed a wearable biosensor technology where conductive ink is literally painted directly onto the skin in custom designs, drying into functional electrodes that can monitor physiological signals. This is a genuinely interesting approach to the wearable sensor problem — most current biosensors are rigid, discrete devices (think: Apple Watch, continuous glucose monitors) that have inherent limitations in where and how they can be placed on the body. A painted electrode can conform perfectly to skin contours, can be applied in any shape or pattern, and could in theory cover much larger body surface areas for richer data collection. The 'colorful custom designs' angle suggests the team is thinking about user acceptance — making something people actually want to wear rather than a clinical-looking medical device. Potential applications include continuous health monitoring, athletic performance tracking, and medical diagnostics for conditions that benefit from multi-site measurements. The obvious challenges are durability (how long does painted-on conductive ink hold up?), waterproofing, and how you get data off the electrodes without wires. This is research-stage technology, but it's a creative approach to a real problem in the wearables space.
