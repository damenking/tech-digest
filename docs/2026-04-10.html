Why AI systems fail quietly — and what to do about it
IEEE Spectrum ran a good engineering-focused piece on a failure mode that anyone running AI in production will recognize: systems that look healthy by every monitoring metric but are silently producing increasingly wrong outputs. The piece describes a scenario where dashboards all show green, no alerts fire, but user-facing decisions are slowly degrading. This is qualitatively different from traditional software failures, which tend to be discrete and detectable — a crashed process, a thrown exception, a timeout. AI model degradation can be gradual, statistical, and invisible to conventional observability tooling. The article discusses the importance of output-level monitoring (not just infrastructure-level), the role of shadow evaluation pipelines, and the challenge of defining ground truth for systems with no single correct answer. For engineers who've inherited responsibility for production AI systems or are building new ones, this is a practically useful read. The core insight — that AI reliability requires a fundamentally different approach to monitoring than traditional software — is one that organizations are still learning to operationalize.

Clinical trial confirms gene editing works for β-Thalassemia — expanding the reach of CRISPR-style therapy
A new clinical trial has demonstrated that gene editing successfully treats β-Thalassemia, a serious inherited blood disorder that affects hemoglobin production and requires lifelong blood transfusions or bone marrow transplants for severe cases. The approach uses an improved gene editing process to reactivate the fetal version of a hemoglobin gene — essentially turning back on a developmental switch that normally closes after birth. This is meaningful because it extends the proven therapeutic approach used for sickle cell disease (where similar fetal hemoglobin reactivation strategies have shown dramatic results) to another major hemoglobinopathy. β-Thalassemia affects hundreds of thousands of people globally, with the highest prevalence in Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, and South Asian populations. The clinical results add to the growing evidence that gene editing is becoming a reliable therapeutic modality, not just a research curiosity. The broader implication is that the same basic mechanism — reactivating fetal hemoglobin — may serve as a platform for treating the entire class of hemoglobin disorders. For anyone following the gene therapy space, this is a solid incremental confirmation that the foundational science is holding up in human trials.

Chip can project video from something the size of a grain of sand — and it matters for quantum computing
IEEE Spectrum published a fascinating piece about a new photonic chip capable of projecting video at a scale previously unachievable — relevant not just as a miniaturization curiosity but as a potential enabler for quantum computing, which requires precise optical control of individual qubits at very small scales. Current quantum computers need millions of qubits to realize their potential in areas like drug discovery, cryptography, and materials science, and one of the hardest engineering challenges is delivering precise light signals to control those qubits without the hardware footprint becoming unmanageable. MEMS (micro-electromechanical systems) photonics at this scale could help solve that packaging problem. The chip uses arrays of tiny metallic cantilevers to steer light with high precision. Beyond quantum computing, ultra-miniaturized projection capability has applications in augmented reality, endoscopic medical imaging, and other fields where you need to get optical output into very tight spaces. It's genuinely early-stage research, so the path from 'grain of sand video projector' to 'deployed quantum control system' is long — but the underlying physics result is noteworthy.
