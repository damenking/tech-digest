For engineers running their own Linux-based virtualization stacks — KVM, QEMU, or otherwise — this is a strong reminder to stay current on kernel patches. City Labs Achieves First Commercial Nuclear Power Milestone in Space with BOHR Mission
Miami-based City Labs has achieved what's being called a first for commercial nuclear power in space with its BOHR mission, described as a pathfinder for future nuclear-powered spacecraft. Nuclear power in space is not new — NASA and the Soviet Union used radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs) for decades on deep space probes — but commercial nuclear power in space is genuinely novel territory. City Labs appears to be working with betavoltaic technology, which generates electricity from the beta decay of radioactive isotopes, rather than traditional reactor-based nuclear fission. The BOHR mission's significance is establishing that a commercial entity, not a government agency, can successfully operate nuclear power technology in the space environment. As ambitions for longer-duration space missions, lunar bases, and deep space exploration grow, nuclear power becomes increasingly important because solar panels lose effectiveness far from the sun. The commercial angle matters because it could dramatically accelerate the development of nuclear space power systems compared to the slow pace of government programs. City Labs is a relatively small company making a big technical claim, so independent verification of what exactly was achieved will be important. If the technology proves viable and scalable, it has enormous implications for the economics of space exploration — power is one of the fundamental constraints on what spacecraft can do.
