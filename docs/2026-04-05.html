Research finds AI users entering 'cognitive surrender,' accepting faulty answers without critical thinking
New research is sounding alarms about a behavioral pattern researchers are calling 'cognitive surrender' — where people using AI tools stop applying their own logical reasoning and simply accept whatever the model outputs, even when it's demonstrably wrong. The experiments showed large majorities of participants uncritically accepting 'faulty' AI answers across a range of tasks. This is distinct from the well-known hallucination problem; this is about user behavior, not model behavior. People are outsourcing their judgment, not just their labor. For senior engineers and knowledge workers, this is a nuanced finding — using AI to accelerate work you already understand is very different from using it to make decisions in domains where you can't verify the output. The research adds empirical weight to concerns that have been largely anecdotal until now: that AI tools might be quietly degrading the critical thinking skills of their users over time. It also has implications for how organizations should deploy AI internally — if employees lose the ability to sanity-check AI outputs, the error propagation risk compounds. The timing is relevant alongside the OpenClaw security story this week: one of the biggest risks of over-trusting AI-generated code is that nobody catches the vulnerabilities before they ship. This won't slow AI adoption, but it should inform how teams structure human oversight workflows.

AI is flooding open source security teams with high-quality vulnerability reports — and it's overwhelming
A striking pattern is emerging in open source security: AI tools are now generating a flood of legitimate, high-quality vulnerability reports that are taxing the capacity of maintainers to respond. Greg Kroah-Hartman, a key Linux kernel maintainer, noted that a month ago something shifted — reports went from obvious AI slop to real, valid security findings at a dramatically higher volume. Daniel Stenberg, the lead developer of cURL, echoed this, saying he's now spending hours per day on the influx. Willy Tarreau reported the Linux kernel security list went from 2-3 reports per week two years ago, to 10 per week last year, to 5-10 per day now. The quality improvement is the crucial development here: previously, AI-generated security reports were easy to dismiss. Now they require genuine triage and response. This is a double-edged situation: on one hand, real vulnerabilities are being found faster, which is good for security. On the other hand, the human review capacity of volunteer-maintained open source projects hasn't scaled proportionally. Security researcher Thomas Ptacek's piece 'Vulnerability Research Is Cooked' frames this as a step-function change in the economics of exploit development, with coding agents poised to make things even more intense in the coming months. For organizations that rely on open source infrastructure — which is essentially everyone — this is a signal to pay closer attention to security advisories and patch cadence, because the pipeline of discovered vulnerabilities is accelerating rapidly.

MIT Technology Review: AI benchmarks are broken — here's what we need instead
MIT Technology Review published a substantive critique of how the AI industry evaluates model performance, arguing that the traditional human-vs-AI comparison framework is fundamentally flawed and increasingly misleading. The core argument is that benchmarking AI against individual humans on isolated tasks with clear metrics creates a seductive but misleading picture of capability — models can ace benchmarks while failing in real-world deployment contexts that require sustained, contextual, multi-stakeholder performance.
