The goal is to put them on notice, restrict their international travel, and potentially trigger sanctions. This is part of a broader pattern of Western law enforcement using naming and shaming, sanctions, and infrastructure seizures as tools against ransomware groups they can't arrest. For security professionals, this is a reminder that attribution in ransomware cases, once thought nearly impossible, has improved dramatically. Whether naming these individuals leads to any practical accountability is uncertain, but it adds to the pressure on the ecosystem and is worth tracking.

Court rules sports prediction market bets are 'swaps,' exempt from state gambling laws
A federal court has ruled that sports bets placed through prediction markets — platforms like Kalshi that frame wagers as financial contracts — qualify as 'swaps' under the Commodity Exchange Act, meaning they're regulated by the CFTC rather than by state gambling regulators. This is a significant ruling because it effectively exempts prediction market sports betting from the patchwork of state gambling laws that govern traditional sportsbooks. The legal argument is that these are financial instruments where you're exchanging risk about future outcomes, not 'gambling' in the traditional sense — a distinction that sounds sophistic but apparently holds up legally. For the prediction market industry, this is a major win: it opens up a national market without needing to negotiate with 50 state gaming commissions, each with their own rules and licensing requirements. Traditional sportsbooks, which have invested heavily in state-by-state licensing, are not going to be happy about a competitor operating under a lighter regulatory regime. The CFTC's jurisdiction over these products also means a different enforcement approach — financial regulators versus gaming regulators have quite different tools and priorities. For technologists, the interesting angle is that this ruling creates space for a new generation of prediction market products that blend financial trading UX with sports betting content. Whether this is actually good for consumers is a genuinely open question — financial product wrappers don't necessarily mean better protections.
