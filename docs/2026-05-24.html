OpenAI model disproves 80-year-old conjecture in discrete geometry
An OpenAI model has solved the unit distance problem, disproving a major conjecture in discrete geometry that had stood for roughly 80 years. This is the kind of result that separates genuine mathematical reasoning from pattern-matching — the model didn't just find a known proof, it constructed a counterexample to something mathematicians had long assumed to be true. Discrete geometry deals with combinatorial properties of geometric objects, and the unit distance problem asks how many pairs of points in a set can be exactly distance 1 apart. The conjecture being disproved had shaped decades of research in the field. OpenAI hasn't released full technical details yet, but this follows a broader trend of AI systems making verifiable contributions to pure mathematics — DeepMind's AlphaProof cracked IMO problems last year, and now we're seeing frontier models tackle open research problems. What's notable here is that this isn't a proof-assistance story where humans guide the system; the model apparently found the result somewhat autonomously. For mathematicians, this is both exciting and slightly unsettling, since it suggests AI might be generating results faster than the field can absorb them. For engineers, it's a useful signal about the gap between LLMs that autocomplete code and reasoning systems that can do original intellectual work. The practical implications for applied math and cryptography could follow, though likely on a longer timeline. Worth paying attention to as a bellwether for AI capability in formal reasoning.

AI used to reconstruct dead pilots' voices from crash investigation spectrograms
People have been using AI to reconstruct the voices of pilots who died in crashes — not from the audio recordings themselves, which are legally protected, but from spectrogram images included in NTSB investigation documents. Spectrograms are visual representations of audio frequency over time, and it turns out modern AI can reverse-engineer reasonably intelligible audio from them. The NTSB temporarily blocked access to its docket system when this came to light, and the US government is now scrambling to figure out how to stop it. The underlying law bans disclosure of cockpit audio recordings specifically because of concerns about how they'd be used — privacy for the deceased, sensitivity to families, and the chilling effect on candid cockpit communications during incidents. The spectrogram workaround flouts the spirit of that law by exploiting a technical gap: the regulations didn't anticipate that a visual representation of audio could be converted back into usable audio with AI. This is a genuinely novel legal and technical problem. It raises broader questions about what counts as 'disclosure' when AI can reconstruct information from derivative representations. For anyone working on data governance, privacy engineering, or compliance, this is a useful case study in how AI capabilities invalidate assumptions baked into older regulatory frameworks. The families of crash victims have obvious standing to be upset about this. The government's options are limited — you can't un-publish spectrograms already in public dockets, and blocking future spectrograms would reduce transparency in crash investigations.

Memory shortage driving significant consumer electronics price increases
A piece flagged by Simon Willison offers what he calls the clearest explanation yet of why consumer products using memory are likely to get significantly more expensive over the next few years. The core issue is that memory manufacturing is now dominated by just three large companies, and they have a fixed wafer capacity that is increasingly being consumed by AI infrastructure — data centers and accelerators need enormous amounts of high-bandwidth memory, and that demand is crowding out the commodity DRAM and NAND that goes into consumer devices. The memory shortage is already visible in pricing trends, and the argument is that it's structural rather than cyclical — it won't resolve when one product cycle ends. For smartphone makers, this means the economics of cheap handsets are breaking down, with entry-level and mid-range devices likely to see the sharpest price increases since low-cost phones have thinner margins to absorb the increase. For developers, this has downstream implications for the devices your users are running — if cheap Android phones get significantly more expensive, the addressable market for mobile apps in price-sensitive regions shrinks. The AI infrastructure build-out is the proximate cause, which creates an interesting irony: the tools being built to democratize AI access are, through memory demand, making the devices people use to access AI more expensive. This is the kind of second-order effect that gets lost in the hype cycle around AI infrastructure investment. Worth watching if you're in hardware, embedded systems, or any business that depends on consumer device penetration.

.NET 11 preview adds union types to C#
The .NET 11 preview 2 has introduced union types to C#, which has generated considerable excitement among .NET developers on Hacker News with a score of 180. Union types — sometimes called discriminated unions or sum types — allow a variable to hold one of several specified types, with the compiler enforcing exhaustive pattern matching. Languages like F#, Rust, Kotlin, Swift, and TypeScript have had some form of this for years, and C# developers have been requesting it for a long time. The addition is significant because it enables safer, more expressive modeling of state machines, result types, and domain models without the boilerplate of class hierarchies or the unsafety of casting. For teams doing domain-driven design in C#, this is a meaningful quality-of-life improvement. The implementation details matter a lot here — union types can be done well or done awkwardly depending on how they interact with the existing type system, generics, and pattern matching syntax. C# 8 through 12 have progressively improved pattern matching, so the groundwork has been laid. This is .NET 11 preview, meaning it's not production-ready yet, but it's a strong signal about where the language is heading. For developers evaluating whether to stay on C# or migrate to F# for functional-style code, this might tip the balance toward staying — though F# will still have more expressive union types for the foreseeable future.
