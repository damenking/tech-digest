The settlement likely includes licensing terms that give Anthropic some legal cover going forward, though the specifics haven't been fully disclosed. This comes at an interesting moment when OpenAI, Google, and Meta all face similar lawsuits from authors, publishers, and news organizations. Other labs will be watching closely to see whether settling early, as Anthropic has done, becomes the playbook, or whether some company decides to fight it out in court and potentially get a more favorable ruling. For the broader industry, the implicit message is that training on copyrighted data has a price tag — and that price tag may start getting baked into the cost of building frontier models. AI is introducing new biases in hiring — and they may be worse than human biases
New research published and covered by MIT Technology Review finds that large language models used for resume screening don't just inherit human biases from their training data — they can also develop entirely novel biases of their own, ones that weren't present in the original human decisions the models were trained on. This is a subtle but important distinction: most discussions of AI bias assume the problem is amplifying existing human prejudice, but this research suggests AI systems can generate new, emergent forms of bias that are harder to detect and attribute. The study's implications are significant given how rapidly AI-based resume screening is being adopted by HR departments trying to handle application volume that humans simply can't process manually. If the hiring AI is creating new categories of discrimination that don't map cleanly to protected classes, it may be difficult to catch through standard fairness audits. Researchers found the biases manifested in patterns related to name characteristics, writing style, and word choice that correlate with demographic factors in ways that are non-obvious. This is exactly the kind of finding that should give enterprise teams pause before deploying off-the-shelf AI hiring tools — not because AI screening is inherently bad, but because the evaluation frameworks most companies use to validate these tools may not be sophisticated enough to catch novel bias patterns. Regulators in the EU and several US states are already looking at AI hiring tools, and this research will almost certainly be cited in upcoming policy discussions. For engineers building or integrating these systems, this is a strong argument for investing in rigorous, demographically-stratified output testing rather than relying on vendors' fairness claims.

OpenAI's GPT-Red: an LLM trained specifically to attack other LLMs
OpenAI has detailed GPT-Red, an automated red-teaming system that uses self-play to stress-test its production models against adversarial attacks, prompt injection, and safety bypasses. The system was used extensively in training GPT-5.6, OpenAI's latest flagship model, and the company says it made the release their most robust against cyberattacks to date. The core idea is clever: rather than relying on human red-teamers (who are expensive, slow, and limited in creativity), GPT-Red is itself an LLM fine-tuned specifically to find and exploit weaknesses in other models, operating in an adversarial self-play loop. This approach automates the discovery of attack vectors at a scale and speed that human teams simply can't match. It's a significant advancement in AI safety methodology, though it's worth noting OpenAI is both the entity building the attack system and the one claiming it makes their models safer — independent verification would be welcome. From a security engineering perspective, this is analogous to using fuzzing and automated vulnerability scanners in software development, applied to model behavior rather than code. The prompt injection angle is particularly relevant for anyone building agentic applications, where malicious inputs can redirect an AI agent to perform unintended actions. OpenAI is framing this as part of their broader safety infrastructure, and it does represent genuine technical progress in the difficult problem of making powerful models more reliably safe. IEEE Spectrum also covered this story with a piece on jailbreaking LLMs, suggesting the adversarial AI security space is getting significant research attention from multiple directions right now.

Ransomware surge pushes governments toward considering payment bans
Ransomware attacks have surged to the point where governments in multiple countries are seriously discussing outright bans on ransom payments, a policy that has been debated for years but is now gaining new momentum. The core logic of a payment ban is straightforward: ransomware only works as a business model because victims pay, so eliminating payments eliminates the incentive. The counterargument — which has historically prevented bans from moving forward — is that banning payments doesn't make attacks stop, it just means victims (hospitals, municipalities, critical infrastructure operators) suffer the damage without any recovery option. The current debate is being shaped by increasingly sophisticated ransomware operations, many tied to nation-state actors or groups operating with state tolerance, that are targeting critical infrastructure with attacks designed to maximize pressure to pay. A ban would be most effective if adopted simultaneously by multiple major economies, otherwise it just disadvantages companies in ban-adopting countries relative to competitors who can still pay to recover. The UK, Australia, and some EU members have been the most vocal about considering bans, with the US in a more ambiguous position. For enterprise security teams, this is a wake-up call to evaluate your incident response and recovery capabilities under the assumption that paying may not be legally permissible in the future — which means investing heavily in backup infrastructure, immutable storage, and recovery procedures now. The Ars Technica piece is worth reading in full for the case studies of recent victims facing this decision.

Previously, reverse-engineering something like a smart home device's local protocol was theoretically possible but rarely worth the effort — it required significant skilled engineering time for something that might only benefit one person, and the undocumented API could break at any time anyway. With AI coding agents that can write, test, and iterate on reverse-engineering code autonomously, the effort required has dropped dramatically. The broader principle here is important: reduced cost of writing code changes which problems are worth solving. A task that took 40 hours of skilled engineering is worth doing for maybe 10 people in the world; a task that takes 2 hours of AI-assisted work is worth doing for essentially anyone who wants it. This has implications well beyond home automation — it applies to any domain where the barrier to custom software development was time and expertise rather than access to information. The security implications are also real: if reverse-engineering is cheap, so is building exploits and custom attack tooling. The same dynamic that empowers hobbyists also empowers adversaries, which is worth keeping in mind when thinking about the net security impact of AI coding capabilities.

Firefighting drones are being tested against wildfires — and the results are promising
California and the XPRIZE Foundation are running serious tests of drone-based early wildfire detection and suppression systems, driven by the grim reality that wildfires now threaten the US nearly year-round rather than being a seasonal problem. The basic premise is that catching a fire in its first few minutes — when it's still small enough to be suppressed by a drone payload — could prevent the kind of catastrophic megafires that have become tragically routine. Current firefighting aircraft (air tankers, helicopters) are expensive to operate, require human pilots, and have significant response time limitations. Autonomous drones can potentially be stationed in high-risk areas, detect fire via thermal imaging or smoke detection, and respond in minutes rather than hours. The XPRIZE competition structure is designed to accelerate development by setting clear performance targets and attracting multiple competing approaches. Early test results appear encouraging for early detection, though suppression at meaningful scale remains a harder problem — a drone's payload capacity is a fraction of an air tanker's. The more realistic near-term value is probably drone systems as first responders that can attack a fire while larger conventional resources are mobilized, rather than as a complete replacement. Given the scale of wildfire damage — billions of dollars annually and significant loss of life — even a modest improvement in early detection and response could have enormous value. This is one of those areas where the technology is genuinely useful and the problem is genuinely important.
