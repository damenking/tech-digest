OpenAI's GPT-Red: An LLM That Hacks Other LLMs to Make Them Safer
OpenAI has published details on GPT-Red, an internal automated red-teaming system it uses to stress-test its models against cyberattacks and adversarial prompts. The system works through self-play — GPT-Red attacks OpenAI's production models, which then train against those attacks, in a continuous loop. GPT-5.6, released last week, was the first flagship model trained extensively against GPT-Red, and OpenAI claims it's their most robust release to date. The core idea isn't entirely new — adversarial training has been a standard ML technique for years — but automating it at the LLM level and integrating it into the production training pipeline is a meaningful step. Traditional red-teaming is slow and expensive because it requires human researchers to manually find exploits; GPT-Red can generate and test attack variations at machine speed. OpenAI published both a blog post and a technical writeup, giving the community something to actually evaluate rather than just marketing claims. The timing is notable: this comes amid a broader industry reckoning with prompt injection, data exfiltration, and agentic AI systems that have elevated surface areas for attack. If the approach works as advertised, it's a reasonable template for how model safety could scale. That said, the classic arms-race concern applies — a smarter red-team model also means smarter attacks, and it's not obvious the defender always wins that loop. Worth watching how third-party researchers respond to the technical claims.

Researcher Finds Data Exfiltration Hole in Claude's Web Fetch Tool
Security researcher Ayush Paul has demonstrated a working exploit that tricks Claude into leaking sensitive user data through its web_fetch tool, despite Anthropic's deliberate design efforts to prevent exactly this class of attack. The web_fetch tool was architected with exfiltration in mind — Anthropic had previously implemented guardrails that made it difficult for a malicious webpage to instruct Claude to send data to an attacker-controlled server. Paul found a gap in that design that allows a crafted page to bypass those protections and exfiltrate information from the conversation. This is a textbook 'lethal trifecta' attack: a model with memory or context, access to external tools, and the ability to make outbound requests. The fact that Anthropic invested specifically in preventing this and a hole was still found should be a loud signal to anyone building agentic systems that this problem space is genuinely hard. Claude is increasingly being used in agentic workflows where it browses the web autonomously, which makes this a real-world risk, not just an academic exercise. The exploit has been responsibly disclosed, and Simon Willison — who has tracked this attack class closely — flagged it as significant. It's a good reminder that AI safety isn't just about harmful outputs; the attack surface of AI systems with tool access is a separate and underappreciated concern. Developers building on top of Claude or similar models with web access should be paying close attention to this.

xAI's Grok CLI Was Silently Uploading Your Entire Home Directory to Google Cloud
xAI's Grok CLI coding agent faced serious community backlash after users discovered that running the tool in a directory caused it to upload the entire contents of that directory to xAI's Google Cloud storage buckets. One user reported accidentally running it in their home directory and watching it silently upload SSH keys, password manager files, and other sensitive data before they could stop it. This is a severe privacy and security failure — the kind of behavior that should have been caught in basic QA, let alone a security review. The tool has since been open-sourced on GitHub as xai-org/grok-build, which is either a goodwill gesture or damage control depending on your level of cynicism. Open-sourcing does allow the community to audit the code and understand exactly what was happening and why. Simon Willison covered both the security incident and the subsequent open-source release in separate posts. The incident puts xAI in uncomfortable company with other tools that have been caught exfiltrating more data than users knew about. For developers, the lesson is depressingly familiar: always run new CLI tools in an isolated, sandboxed directory — never your home folder — until you've audited what they actually do. The broader concern is that as AI coding agents proliferate, the attack surface for accidental or intentional data collection expands dramatically. xAI needs to be much more explicit about its data collection practices before asking developers to trust Grok with their codebases.

PsiQuantum's Plan to Build a Massive Photonic Quantum Computer
PsiQuantum has detailed its roadmap for building what it believes could be the world's first genuinely useful large-scale quantum computer, and the approach is distinctive: it uses photons — particles of light — rather than superconducting qubits or trapped ions, which are the dominant approaches from IBM, Google, and IonQ. The machine will live in a room resembling a data center crossed with an ice cream factory, with roughly 100 stainless-steel cabinets each cooled to just a few degrees above absolute zero using liquid helium. Photonic quantum computing has a fundamental advantage in that light-based qubits are naturally more resistant to decoherence — the tendency of quantum states to collapse due to environmental interference — which has been the central engineering obstacle for the field. PsiQuantum has been unusually quiet compared to competitors who announce milestone after milestone, preferring to work toward a single large-scale machine rather than iterating on smaller NISQ-era devices. This all-or-nothing approach is a gamble, but the argument is that small quantum computers aren't useful enough to matter — you need scale to achieve quantum advantage on real problems. The company has raised substantial funding and has partnerships with GlobalFoundries for chip fabrication, which gives it a credible path to actually manufacturing components at scale. MIT Technology Review's coverage gives this serious treatment, suggesting the technical claims warrant attention. For engineers tracking the quantum computing space, PsiQuantum represents the most credible photonic approach and a genuine alternative thesis to the superconducting qubit mainstream.

Anthropic's 'Jacobian Lens' Gives Clearest View Yet Into Claude's Internal Reasoning
Anthropic has developed a technique called the Jacobian lens that gives researchers an unusually direct view into what's happening inside Claude as it reasons through problems — what the company describes as a window into the model's 'internal thoughts.' The findings range from unsurprising to genuinely unnerving, according to MIT Technology Review's coverage. Interpretability research like this is Anthropic's signature contribution to the AI safety field — they've consistently invested more in understanding model internals than competitors who focus primarily on capability and alignment through RLHF. The Jacobian lens works by analyzing the mathematical relationships between internal representations as the model processes a query, revealing conceptual structures that weren't directly trained for. What's notable is that Anthropic found coherent conceptual spaces — areas where related concepts cluster together — which has implications for both understanding and potentially controlling model behavior. The 'doesn't show' caveat in MIT TR's headline is important: finding that a model has internal structure doesn't prove it's conscious, sentient, or has genuine understanding in any philosophically meaningful sense. The interpretability research community has been cautious about overclaiming from these findings. Still, as a technical tool for diagnosing model behavior and potentially catching misalignment before it manifests in outputs, this is meaningful progress. It also matters for debugging — if you can trace why a model gave a particular answer through its internal representations, you have a much stronger foundation for fixing problems than black-box output analysis.
