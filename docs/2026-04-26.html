Anthropic hasn't published full results yet, but the fact that they built and ran this at all signals they're serious about understanding multi-agent commerce as a real use case. For developers building agentic systems, this is relevant background: the plumbing for agent-to-agent transactions is apparently mature enough for Anthropic to run real-money experiments. University subdomain hijacking: hundreds of .edu domains serving spam and worse
A security investigation revealed that hundreds of subdomains belonging to dozens of major universities have been hijacked by scammers, who are using the trusted .edu domain authority to serve spam, scam sites, and reportedly pornographic content. The mechanism is straightforward: universities frequently set up subdomains for projects, events, or departments and then forget to clean them up when those projects end. Dangling DNS records pointing to services that no longer exist can be claimed by anyone who spins up the right hosting — a technique called subdomain takeover. The .edu TLD is particularly valuable for attackers because search engines and spam filters historically trust it more than commercial domains. The scale here — hundreds of subdomains across dozens of institutions — suggests this isn't targeted attacks but systematic scanning for vulnerable configurations. For university IT departments, the remediation is straightforward but tedious: audit all DNS records, identify dangling entries, and delete them. The challenge is that large universities have complex, decentralized IT structures where individual departments manage their own subdomains and nobody has a complete picture. This is a good reminder that DNS hygiene is not a one-time task but an ongoing operational responsibility, and that domain authority is an asset that can be stolen through neglect rather than direct attack.
