IBM Unveils Sub-1nm Chip Technology That Could Extend Moore's Law a Decade
IBM has demonstrated a prototype chip packing roughly 100 billion transistors into a fingernail-sized area, achieving transistor density that crosses below the 1-nanometer node threshold — a first, according to IBM. That's roughly twice the density of their previous state-of-the-art chip announced in 2021. The underlying technology involves a new architectural approach IBM is calling a 'block of flats' design (the BBC's colorful description), which stacks functional layers vertically rather than continuing to shrink horizontal feature sizes. This matters because the traditional path of shrinking transistors on a flat plane has been running into fundamental physical limits for years — what Moore's Law actually requires is finding new ways to pack more compute into the same space, and vertical integration is one of the more promising directions. IBM's announcement is a research prototype, not a production chip — the BBC notes it will be some time before this reaches manufacturing. But prototypes at this density level tend to telegraph what TSMC and Intel roadmaps will look like in 5-7 years. The implications for AI compute are significant: denser chips mean more operations per watt, which is the key constraint in data center scaling right now. MIT Technology Review's coverage notes this could help extend Moore's Law another decade, which would be a meaningful reprieve for an industry increasingly worried about the end of easy compute scaling.

Antibiotic 'Megacluster' Discovery Opens New Strategy Against Drug-Resistant Superbugs
Researchers have announced the discovery of an antibiotic 'megacluster' — a large collection of biosynthetic gene clusters in bacteria that appear to encode novel antibiotic compounds. The finding is being described by experts as 'an exciting advance in efforts to restock the antibiotic arsenal,' which is notable understated scientific language for something genuinely significant. The antibiotic pipeline has been in crisis for decades: developing new antibiotics is expensive, the drugs are typically used for short courses (limiting commercial return), and resistance emerges quickly after deployment. Meanwhile, drug-resistant bacteria (MRSA, CRE, and others) are a growing cause of death globally — the WHO has flagged antimicrobial resistance as one of the top global public health threats. Megaclusters are interesting because they suggest a new screening strategy: instead of looking for individual antibiotic compounds one at a time, researchers may be able to identify and mine entire clusters of related compounds that could yield multiple drug candidates. This is analogous to how genome sequencing changed drug discovery by letting researchers search computationally rather than empirically. The practical timeline from discovery to approved drug is long (10+ years, typically), so this isn't imminent relief. But new screening approaches that expand the candidate pool are genuinely valuable when the existing pipeline is as thin as it currently is.
