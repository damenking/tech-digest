Firefox and SpiderMonkey are saying goodbye to Asm.js — an end of an era
The SpiderMonkey team (Firefox's JavaScript engine) announced they are deprecating and removing support for Asm.js, the low-level JavaScript subset that was Mozilla's precursor to WebAssembly. Asm.js was a clever hack from around 2013 that allowed near-native performance by using a strict subset of JavaScript that could be ahead-of-time compiled — it was how Emscripten first brought C/C++ code to the browser before WebAssembly existed. The decision to drop it makes complete engineering sense: WebAssembly has fully superseded Asm.js, browser support is universal, and maintaining Asm.js-specific optimization paths in the engine is pure maintenance overhead. In practice, almost nothing targeting modern browsers is intentionally writing Asm.js — it mostly survives as legacy output from old Emscripten builds. The Hacker News score of 144 reflects a mix of nostalgia and engineering pragmatism: people who were there remember what a genuinely impressive technical achievement Asm.js was at the time. For the very small number of production systems still relying on Asm.js output, this is a migration trigger — update your Emscripten toolchain or WebAssembly compilation pipeline. The broader significance is historical: Asm.js was a proof of concept that directly motivated the creation of WebAssembly as a proper W3C standard, making it one of those transitional technologies that succeeded by making itself obsolete.
