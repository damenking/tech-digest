Quantum cryptography pioneers Bennett and Brassard win the Turing Award
Charles H. Bennett and Gilles Brassard have been awarded the 2026 ACM Turing Award for their foundational work in quantum information science and quantum key distribution. Their BB84 protocol, developed in 1984, established the theoretical and practical basis for quantum cryptography — the ability to distribute encryption keys in a way that is provably secure against eavesdropping, guaranteed by the laws of physics rather than computational hardness assumptions. This matters now more than ever because the threat of 'harvest now, decrypt later' attacks — where adversaries collect encrypted traffic today intending to decrypt it once quantum computers are powerful enough to break classical encryption — is real and actively being planned for by intelligence agencies. Bennett and Brassard's work is the foundation for quantum key distribution networks that are being deployed in some high-security contexts today. The Turing Award is computing's Nobel Prize equivalent, so this is the field's highest recognition. BBC Technology noted that their work is hoped to make digital communications secure for decades ahead — which is accurate, though practical large-scale deployment of QKD remains limited by infrastructure costs and distance constraints. For security engineers, this is a good moment to revisit what post-quantum cryptography preparedness looks like for your systems.
