Anthropic Finds 'Hidden Space' Where Claude Reasons — New Research Has Unsettling Implications
Anthropic has published research using a new tool called the 'Jacobian lens' that gives the clearest picture yet of what's actually happening inside large language models as they process queries. What they found ranges from the mundane to, in MIT Technology Review's words, the unnerving — the model appears to have an internal reasoning space where it 'puzzles over concepts' before producing output, distinct from the token-by-token generation we usually think of. This is a significant mechanistic interpretability result because it suggests LLMs have something more like a scratchpad or working memory than a simple next-token prediction machine, which has implications for how we think about model alignment and safety. Anthropic has a well-earned reputation for publishing rigorous and sometimes strange research — they've previously investigated whether AI models can experience something like pain — and this work continues that tradition. The practical implication for AI safety is substantial: if you can see where the model is 'thinking,' you might be able to detect deception, misalignment, or reasoning failures before they manifest as bad outputs. MIT Technology Review notes that Anthropic is currently the world's most valuable AI company at a nearly $1 trillion valuation, which gives it the resources to pursue this kind of fundamental research that competitors might deprioritize. The result also somewhat validates the intuition many researchers have had that chain-of-thought prompting works because it externalizes a process that was already happening internally. For engineers building on top of Claude, this research hints at future APIs that might expose intermediate reasoning states. Whether this translates into meaningfully safer or more controllable models in the short term is an open question, but it's the kind of foundational work the field needs.

CISA Warns: Russian State Hackers Are Actively Targeting Home Routers for Residential Proxies
The US Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency has issued a warning that Russian state-sponsored hackers are actively targeting residential routers to build networks of residential proxies — IP addresses that appear to belong to ordinary home users rather than data centers. This is a significant operational security concern because residential proxies are extremely effective at evading detection: when malicious traffic originates from a legitimate home ISP IP, it bypasses many corporate and government network defenses that would flag datacenter traffic. The technique has become increasingly popular among sophisticated threat actors precisely because it's hard to block without also blocking legitimate users. For home users, the attack surface is your router's admin interface, particularly if you're using default credentials, haven't applied firmware updates, or are running older hardware that no longer receives security patches. For enterprise security teams, this warning should prompt a review of how you handle traffic from residential IP ranges — the old assumption that residential IP = legitimate user is no longer safe. The targeting of home infrastructure reflects a broader trend in state-sponsored hacking: rather than attacking hardened enterprise targets directly, adversaries are increasingly building persistent footholds in the soft underbelly of consumer devices that sit adjacent to enterprise networks (through VPNs, home offices, etc.). CISA's specific call for router users to be 'vigilant' is unfortunately vague — the practical steps are: change default credentials, enable automatic updates if available, and consider whether your router model is still supported by the manufacturer. This warning lands in a context where post-COVID remote work has permanently blurred the line between home and enterprise network security.

Lithium recovery efficiency is one of the key bottlenecks in the EV battery supply chain: lithium is geographically concentrated (mostly in South America and Australia), subject to geopolitical supply risk, and its price volatility has been a major factor in EV cost instability. The technical challenge in lithium recovery is that it's present in relatively low concentrations within battery chemistry and tends to bond with other materials during cycling, making clean separation energy-intensive and expensive. Whether this 90% figure holds at commercial scale and what the energy and cost economics look like will determine if this moves from lab result to industry practice.
