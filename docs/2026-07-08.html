Facing US export controls, China's DeepSeek plans to develop its own chips
DeepSeek, the Chinese AI lab that rattled the industry earlier this year with its efficient frontier models, is now reportedly planning to build its own chips — a direct response to tightening US export controls that have cut off access to Nvidia's high-end GPUs. The plan is early-stage but the strategic intent is clear: reduce dependency on both Nvidia and Huawei, China's primary domestic chip supplier, by developing proprietary silicon. The broader context here is that US export controls have been escalating, and the Biden-era restrictions have been maintained and in some cases tightened under the current administration. China's semiconductor ecosystem still lags significantly behind TSMC-manufactured chips, so this is a long road. But DeepSeek has surprised the industry before. If they can pull off even a moderately capable custom accelerator, it would further reduce the leverage the US holds via chip supply chains. For Western AI labs and policymakers, this is a reminder that export controls are a temporary friction, not a permanent barrier, when the incentive to work around them is this strong.

The dynamic is straightforward — data centers are being built at scale in states like Ohio, Indiana, and Pennsylvania that have existing power infrastructure, and they're consuming enormous amounts of grid capacity, causing prices to rise for everyone connected to the same grid. This creates a genuine policy contradiction: the same administration pushing AI infrastructure investment is also promising to revive domestic manufacturing, and these two goals are competing for the same resource. Utilities are struggling to build new generation capacity fast enough to serve both constituencies. Data centers are generally higher-margin customers and often sign long-term power purchase agreements, making them attractive to utilities even at the expense of industrial customers. There's also a jobs argument here that cuts both ways: data centers employ far fewer people per megawatt consumed than traditional manufacturing. This tension is going to force some genuinely hard policy choices at the state and federal level about how to allocate grid capacity and who gets priority access to power. It's a story worth watching as it will likely influence AI infrastructure siting decisions and potentially trigger new energy legislation.

The practical implementation challenge is significant: robust age verification at the app store level requires collecting sensitive identity data from every user, creating privacy risks and friction that could suppress downloads broadly, not just for minors. Big Tech's 'censorship' framing is partly legitimate and partly self-interested — they don't want the compliance burden or the liability exposure. Texas is part of a broader state-level wave of legislation targeting minors' online access, with similar laws passing in multiple states. For developers, this adds another layer of uncertainty to the already complex app store ecosystem. EU mandatory driver monitoring cameras required in all new cars sold in Europe
Starting now, every new car sold in the European Union must include a driver monitoring camera system — a significant mandate that has received relatively little mainstream tech coverage despite its broad implications. The regulation is part of the EU's General Safety Regulation framework and is aimed at reducing accidents caused by distraction and drowsiness. These systems use inward-facing cameras and computer vision to detect whether the driver is paying attention, and can issue warnings or intervene in some vehicle control systems. The privacy implications are substantial: these cameras are recording driver behavior continuously, and the data retention, processing, and access policies vary by manufacturer and aren't well standardized. Security researchers have already flagged concerns about the attack surface these systems create. For the automotive industry, this is a design constraint that needs to be integrated into every vehicle sold in the EU market, which given shared global platforms means it will show up in cars worldwide. The Hacker News community has been engaged with this story, and the concerns being raised are legitimate — who owns the footage, can it be subpoenaed, can insurers access it, and what happens when these systems are hacked? The regulation reflects a genuine road safety goal, but the implementation details around data governance need far more scrutiny than they've received.
