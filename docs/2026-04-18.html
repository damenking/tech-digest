'Tokenmaxxing' Is Making Developers Less Productive Than They Think
There's a growing pattern in AI-assisted development that TechCrunch is calling 'tokenmaxxing' — essentially, developers feeding enormous amounts of context to AI models and generating massive amounts of code, but then spending disproportionate time reviewing, rewriting, and debugging that code because its quality doesn't match its volume. The core insight is uncomfortable: more tokens consumed does not equal more work done, and the economics of AI coding assistance look much worse once you account for the rewriting overhead and the cognitive load of reviewing AI-generated code you didn't write. This connects to a broader debate about where AI coding tools actually provide leverage — they're clearly useful for boilerplate, for explaining unfamiliar codebases, and for generating tests, but the 'just prompt your way to a feature' workflow may be generating technical debt faster than it's generating features. The Hacker News post 'Spending 3 months coding by hand' (160 points) is clearly adjacent to this conversation — developers are actively questioning whether AI-assisted workflows are improving their craft or degrading it. For senior engineers, this is worth taking seriously: if you're managing teams that have fully embraced AI coding tools, the output metrics (lines of code, features shipped) may be flattering while the underlying codebase quality and maintainability are deteriorating. The tokenmaxxing phenomenon also has real cost implications — API costs for frontier models are not trivial at scale, and if the code requires heavy revision anyway, the ROI math gets questionable. This doesn't mean AI coding tools aren't useful; it means the workflow optimizations that actually work are more nuanced than 'dump everything into context and accept the output.'

NIST Effectively Gives Up on Enriching Most CVEs
A significant and underreported infrastructure story: NIST has announced it is stepping back from its role of enriching most CVE (Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures) entries in the National Vulnerability Database — the authoritative public database that the entire security industry relies on to understand, prioritize, and respond to disclosed vulnerabilities. For context, NIST's NVD enrichment process takes raw CVE identifiers and adds CVSS severity scores, affected product lists, and other metadata that tools like vulnerability scanners, patch management systems, and SIEMs depend on to function. Without that enrichment, a CVE is just a number with a description. The security industry has been noticing processing backlogs at NVD for over a year; this announcement formalizes what was already becoming functionally true. The downstream effects are significant: automated vulnerability management workflows that rely on NVD data will become less reliable, and organizations will need to source enrichment from commercial vendors or other sources. Risky.biz covered this with appropriate alarm — it's a quiet degradation of critical public internet infrastructure that most people won't notice until something breaks. The root cause appears to be funding and prioritization constraints rather than any technical limitation. This is a story about what happens when important but unsexy government-maintained infrastructure gets deprioritized — the CVE/NVD ecosystem is genuinely foundational to how the industry manages security at scale, and its quiet deterioration should be getting more attention than it is.
