US Government Forces Anthropic to Pull Fable 5 and Mythos Models Offline
This is the big one today. The Trump administration's Commerce Department issued an export control directive ordering Anthropic to suspend all access to its Fable 5 and Mythos 5 models for any foreign national — including foreign-national employees working inside the United States. In practice, Anthropic says the directive is so broad that it would be 'operationally impossible' to comply with while keeping the models running, so it has taken them fully offline for everyone. The stated rationale is that the government believes it has identified a method of jailbreaking Fable 5 that poses a national security threat, though no specifics about the vulnerability have been made public. Anthropic pushed back hard in a public statement, writing that it 'disagrees that the finding of a narrow potential jailbreak should be cause for recalling a commercial model deployed to hundreds of millions of people.' The company has been notably candid in its safety communications over the years, and the bitter irony here — widely noted — is that its own transparency about model risks may have handed the government the ammunition to do this. This is the first time the US government has used export control authority to force a major AI lab to take a commercial product offline, which sets a precedent that has the entire industry watching nervously. It also raises an obvious question: if a jailbreak is the threshold for government shutdown, virtually every frontier model is potentially vulnerable. The directive affects Anthropic's most capable models at a time when competitors like OpenAI and Google are aggressively pushing ahead. Simon Willison called it 'nuts,' which feels about right — the scope of the action seems wildly disproportionate to the stated concern, and the lack of any public technical detail makes it very hard to evaluate whether the threat is real or manufactured.

FISA Section 702 Expires, But the Surveillance Continues Until March 2027
Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, which authorizes the NSA to collect communications of foreign targets — and, controversially, incidentally sweeps up Americans' communications — officially expired last night without Congress passing a reauthorization. However, that doesn't mean the spying stops: existing certifications under Section 702 remain legally valid until they expire, which won't happen until March 2027. This is a pattern that has played out before with controversial surveillance authorities — the legal and technical infrastructure of surveillance tends to outlast its explicit legislative authorization. Privacy advocates have argued for years that Section 702 collection is used as a backdoor to surveil Americans without a warrant, since the government can query databases of incidentally collected US person communications without seeking a warrant. The expiration without reauthorization is technically a political win for civil liberties groups, but the practical impact in the near term is essentially zero. For the tech industry, this matters because Section 702 is a primary legal basis for government access to data held by US tech companies on foreign users. The ongoing uncertainty about the law's status creates compliance headaches for companies operating internationally, particularly in the EU where data transfer mechanisms depend partly on the robustness of US surveillance law limitations.

DiffusionGemma: Google's Diffusion-Based Language Model Claims 4x Faster Text Generation
Google DeepMind released DiffusionGemma, a language model based on diffusion methods rather than the standard autoregressive (token-by-token) generation approach. The headline claim is 4x faster text generation, which would be a meaningful practical improvement if it holds up in real-world use cases. Diffusion models for text have been an active research area for a few years, with the appeal being that they can potentially generate text in parallel rather than sequentially, which is the fundamental speed bottleneck in autoregressive models. Simon Willison noted that Google briefly released an experimental Gemini Diffusion model last year that was clocked at 857 tokens per second, which was genuinely impressive. The key question with diffusion language models has always been quality — early versions tended to lag behind autoregressive models on coherence and accuracy, particularly for longer outputs. DiffusionGemma appears to be a more mature version of that earlier research. If diffusion-based text generation can match autoregressive quality at significantly lower latency, it would be a genuinely important advance for deployment economics and real-time applications. Worth watching to see independent evaluations of quality versus the speed claims.
