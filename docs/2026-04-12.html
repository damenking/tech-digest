Anthropic holds back Claude Mythos, restricts it to security researchers under Project Glasswing
Anthropic announced Project Glasswing, a restricted access program under which their newest model — Claude Mythos — is being made available only to a select group of security researchers rather than the general public. The model is described as similar in general capability to Claude Opus 4.6, but its cybersecurity research abilities are apparently potent enough that Anthropic decided a broad release would be irresponsible. This is a genuinely unusual move: most AI labs race to ship and then figure out the safety implications later. Anthropic is doing the opposite here, essentially treating the model's offensive security capabilities as a reason to pause public deployment entirely. A system card PDF was published so the research community can understand what the model can and can't do, which is a reasonable transparency gesture even if you can't actually use the thing. The name 'Glasswing' refers to a butterfly with transparent wings — presumably a metaphor for openness within constraints. This comes at a moment when the broader industry debate around AI and cybersecurity is heating up, partly because researchers have been demonstrating that even smaller models can find serious vulnerabilities. The decision will likely draw both praise from safety advocates and criticism from researchers who feel restricted access slows legitimate defensive security work. It also implicitly validates the concern that frontier models are approaching a threshold where their capabilities in dangerous domains outpace our ability to safely deploy them. Worth watching whether other labs follow suit or use this as a PR opportunity to contrast their own 'open' approach.

This is a significant challenge to the logic underlying Project Glasswing: if the threat surface already exists with models anyone can download and run locally, then gating the frontier model doesn't close the door, it just means defenders don't have access to the best tool while attackers work with what's available. The piece feeds into a long-running debate about whether capability restrictions on frontier models are security theater or meaningful harm reduction. The counterargument is that even marginal capability differences matter a lot at the tail — the worst-case exploits enabled by a much more capable model might be qualitatively different, not just quantitatively. But the empirical claim that smaller models replicate the findings is hard to dismiss without seeing the actual comparison data. For security engineers, this matters practically: it suggests the attack surface from AI-assisted vulnerability discovery is already quite broad. It also puts AI labs in an awkward position — if smaller open models can do the same damage, the 'responsible' labs restricting their frontier models are just handicapping the defense side. This will likely be cited heavily in policy discussions about AI export controls and capability thresholds.

How researchers broke top AI agent benchmarks — and what it means for the field
A Berkeley research group published a detailed post-mortem on how leading AI agent benchmarks were gamed, broken, or otherwise rendered unreliable — and what the field should do next. The piece comes from UC Berkeley's RDI (Research, Development, and Innovation) group, which has been doing serious work on trustworthy evaluation. The core finding is that current agent benchmarks are sufficiently brittle that dedicated effort can produce systems that score extremely well without the underlying capabilities the benchmarks are supposed to measure. This is a known problem in ML evaluation but it's especially acute for agents, where the tasks are longer-horizon, the evaluation is more complex, and the temptation to overfit is higher. The implications are significant: a lot of investment decisions, product roadmaps, and policy discussions are being made on the basis of benchmark numbers that may not reflect real-world capability. The paper proposes a set of principles for more trustworthy evaluations, including greater randomization, holdout test sets, and third-party auditing. For engineers building on top of these models, it's a useful reminder to trust your own evals over vendor-provided ones. The post has generated substantial discussion on Hacker News, suggesting it's hitting a nerve in the practitioner community. This is exactly the kind of methodological work the field needs more of, even if it's uncomfortable for labs who've been marketing benchmark results.

NASA's Artemis II mission has successfully concluded, with all four astronauts splashing down safely in the Pacific Ocean off the coast of San Diego. The mission was the first crewed lunar flyby since Apollo 17 in 1972, and the crew traveled farther from Earth than any humans before them — a genuinely historic milestone that got somewhat less attention than it deserved given everything else happening in the news cycle. NASA's post-mission statement that 'the work ahead is greater than the work behind us' is candid acknowledgment that Artemis II was a transit mission, not a landing — the actual Moon landing is still ahead. The mission's success puts Artemis III, which aims to put humans on the lunar surface for the first time since Apollo, on firmer footing. There are still significant open questions about the Human Landing System (SpaceX's Starship-derived lander) and the spacesuits needed for surface operations. The geopolitical context is worth noting: China's lunar program is advancing rapidly, and NASA has explicitly framed Artemis as partly a competition to establish human presence on the Moon before China does. For the commercial space ecosystem, a successful Artemis II also validates the infrastructure and partnerships that will support future missions. It's a genuine achievement worth celebrating even if the program's cost structure remains a legitimate concern.

This is a meaningful quality-of-life improvement for anyone doing schema migrations in SQLite-backed applications. Simon Willison, who maintains sqlite-utils and has workarounds for exactly this limitation, highlighted it as a significant gap finally being filled. SQLite's release cadence and backward compatibility record remain exemplary — it's one of the most widely deployed software libraries in the world and manages to improve without breaking things, which is genuinely hard to do at that scale. The new Query Result Formatter library (also compiled to WebAssembly) adds flexible rendering options for SQL result tables, which is useful for applications that surface query results in different formats. For developers who've been dismissing SQLite as a 'toy database,' the past few years of development have added features — JSON support, full-text search improvements, WAL mode refinements — that make it a serious choice for a much wider range of applications than it was a decade ago. The 3.53.0 release continues that trajectory.
