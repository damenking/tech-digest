Pentagon Plans to Let AI Companies Train on Classified Military Data
The Pentagon is developing secure environments — essentially classified computational sandboxes — where AI companies like Anthropic and OpenAI can train military-specific versions of their models on classified data. This goes significantly beyond current deployments, where models like Claude are used in read-only question-answering roles in classified settings. Applications already in use reportedly include analyzing targets in Iran, which should give everyone pause about the pace of this integration. The plan would allow the underlying model weights to actually be shaped by classified information, creating purpose-built military AI rather than general-purpose AI applied to military tasks. This is a meaningful architectural distinction: a model trained on classified ops data versus one merely prompted with it has very different security, accountability, and dual-use implications. MIT Technology Review broke this story based on a defense official with direct knowledge, lending it significant credibility. The timing is notable given the ongoing fight between the Pentagon and Anthropic over usage restrictions (see related story). It also raises thorny questions about what happens to those model weights — can they be audited, recalled, or do they become classified assets themselves? For engineers thinking about AI governance, this is the scenario that alignment researchers have been warning about: AI capability deployment outpacing the policy frameworks meant to govern it. There's no public oversight mechanism described here, and Congress has not been mentioned as a stakeholder in this process.

World ID Wants to Cryptographically Tether Every AI Agent to a Human
Tools for Humanity's World ID project is pitching a new use case: attaching an iris-scan-backed cryptographic identity to AI agents, so that every agent operating online can be traced back to a unique human principal. The core problem they're solving is real and growing — as AI agents proliferate, it becomes increasingly difficult to distinguish human activity from automated agent swarms, and the asymmetry of cost (agents are cheap to deploy at scale) creates serious risks for online systems like APIs, forums, and marketplaces. World ID's approach is to issue cryptographic tokens derived from iris scans that prove a specific human is 'behind' a given agent without revealing who that human is — a zero-knowledge approach to humanness attestation. This is technically interesting because it separates identity (you are a real human) from identification (we know who you are), which addresses some privacy concerns while still providing accountability. The practical challenges are enormous: iris scanning infrastructure is not widely deployed, there are serious concerns about biometric data centralization, and Sam Altman's involvement with Worldcoin continues to generate skepticism about the project's true motivations. That said, the underlying problem — how do you know if you're interacting with an agent or a human, and who is responsible for that agent's actions — is one that the industry genuinely needs to solve. Current approaches like CAPTCHAs are increasingly ineffective against capable AI systems. For developers building multi-agent systems, the question of principal attribution (who authorized this action?) is a live engineering problem right now. Whether iris scans are the right solution is another matter, but it's worth watching as the agent ecosystem matures.

Security researchers have disclosed vulnerabilities in IP-based KVM (keyboard-video-mouse) switches from four different manufacturers, covering a category of device that provides remote, out-of-band access to servers — including at the BIOS level, before the operating system loads. This is a particularly sensitive category because IP KVMs are used precisely when other remote access methods fail, meaning they're often deployed in high-security or high-availability environments where their compromise would be most damaging. The disclosure across four manufacturers simultaneously suggests either a coordinated research effort or that there are common underlying vulnerabilities in how these devices are implemented — potentially in shared firmware components or protocols. For data center operators and sysadmins, the immediate action items are to inventory all IP KVM devices, check for firmware updates from their specific vendors, and verify that these devices are not directly internet-accessible without VPN or bastion host protection. The broader lesson is that out-of-band management infrastructure is consistently under-secured relative to its risk level — it's often deprioritized in security reviews because it's 'just for emergencies.' This disclosure should prompt a fresh look at that assumption.

Ryugu Asteroid Samples Contain All DNA and RNA Building Blocks
Analysis of samples returned from the Ryugu asteroid by JAXA's Hayabusa2 mission has revealed the presence of all five nucleobases — the chemical building blocks of DNA and RNA. This is a landmark finding in astrobiology and the study of life's origins because it provides evidence that the chemical precursors to life can form in space and be delivered to planets via asteroids. Previous analyses of Ryugu samples had already found amino acids and other organic compounds, but the complete set of nucleobases is particularly significant because it closes a gap in the panspermia hypothesis — the idea that life's ingredients (or perhaps life itself) can travel between planetary bodies. The nucleobases found include adenine, guanine, cytosine, thymine, and uracil — exactly the set used by Earth life in its genetic code. Finding all five in an uncontaminated extraterrestrial sample is strong evidence that these compounds form through abiotic chemical processes rather than being exclusively products of biology. This doesn't prove that life itself came from space, but it does suggest that wherever the right conditions exist, the chemistry of life may emerge more readily than previously thought. For researchers working on origin-of-life questions, this is significant empirical data from a pristine source — Ryugu samples were collected and returned in sealed containers specifically to prevent terrestrial contamination. The finding earned 215 points on Hacker News, reflecting genuine public interest in the astrobiology implications. It also validates the scientific value of sample-return missions as opposed to remote sensing alone.
