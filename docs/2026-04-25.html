University subdomain hijacking is serving porn and scam content — poor DNS hygiene to blame
Hundreds of subdomains belonging to dozens of major universities have been hijacked by scammers and are now serving pornographic and scam content, according to an Ars Technica investigation. The root cause is shoddy DNS housekeeping: when universities retire web services, they often forget to remove the DNS records pointing to external hosting providers. Attackers identify these dangling DNS records, spin up content on the underlying hosting platform, and effectively commandeer the university's subdomain to serve malicious or embarrassing content. This technique, known as subdomain takeover, is a well-documented attack class that has been around for years — the novelty here is the scale at which it's apparently being exploited against academic institutions. The real harm is twofold: the university's domain reputation is damaged, and malicious actors gain the trust benefit of appearing to serve content from a .edu domain, which many security filters treat as implicitly trustworthy. For any organization managing complex web infrastructure — which includes essentially every university — this is a concrete reminder to audit DNS records regularly, particularly for subdomains pointing to cloud services, CDNs, or third-party SaaS products that may have been decommissioned. Automated tooling exists to scan for dangling CNAME and A records. The fix is straightforward in principle but requires operational discipline to implement at scale across institutions with thousands of subdomains.

A White House memo from Michael Kratsios, the administration's science and technology adviser, formally accuses firms — primarily in China — of wrongfully 'distilling' US AI models. Model distillation involves using outputs from a more capable model to train a smaller, cheaper model, effectively transferring knowledge without licensing it. This has been a persistent concern in the AI industry, since API access to frontier models makes it technically feasible to generate training data at scale. The memo signals that the administration is preparing to take policy action, potentially including export controls or restrictions on API access for certain users. This is a thorny policy area because distillation is also a legitimate technique used by everyone in the industry — the line between acceptable use and theft is blurry and depends heavily on the terms of service and the specific methodology used. For AI companies, the memo validates their concerns but also creates pressure to implement more aggressive usage monitoring and access controls. DeepSeek's efficiency gains have fueled speculation about whether their models incorporate knowledge distilled from OpenAI or other US labs, though DeepSeek denies this. The policy response, whatever form it takes, will need to grapple with the fundamental challenge that model outputs are not currently protected by copyright in most jurisdictions. This is a space where law, policy, and technical reality are significantly misaligned.
