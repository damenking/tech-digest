First ransomware family confirmed to use post-quantum cryptography
Researchers have confirmed the first ransomware family using post-quantum cryptography (PQC) — specifically, algorithms designed to resist attacks from quantum computers. The immediate practical question is: why bother? Quantum computers capable of breaking current encryption don't exist yet, and ransomware operators are typically focused on near-term payoffs, not future-proofing their extortion infrastructure. Ars Technica's coverage digs into this puzzle and the most likely answer is that the ransomware developers are technically sophisticated actors who may be positioning for longevity, or potentially signaling technical capability to demand higher ransoms. There's also a 'harvest now, decrypt later' angle — if encrypted data stolen today could theoretically be decrypted by a future quantum computer, PQC-encrypted ransomware payloads remain undecryptable even in that scenario, which matters for long-term operations. From a defender's perspective, this is mostly a curiosity today since PQC doesn't change the fundamental ransomware dynamic — you still need to prevent the attack and maintain good backups. But it does suggest ransomware groups have developers capable of implementing cutting-edge cryptography, which raises the overall sophistication bar. It's also a preview of a transition the entire industry needs to make anyway, just for different reasons. Security teams should be aware of this development but shouldn't panic — the real-world impact right now is minimal.

Carbon nanotube wiring gets closer to competing with copper
Researchers have made meaningful progress on carbon nanotube (CNT) wiring conductivity, getting it closer to competing with copper — a milestone that the materials science community has been working toward for decades. The catch, as Ars Technica's coverage notes, is that the current material degrades over time, meaning it's not production-ready but it points toward a viable path. Carbon nanotubes have long been theoretically promising for wiring because they can carry higher current densities than copper, are lighter, and have better thermal properties — all of which matter enormously as chip densities increase and interconnect performance becomes a key bottleneck. The degradation problem is the longstanding obstacle: CNT conductors that work initially but degrade in air or under thermal stress aren't useful for electronics manufacturing. This latest result is incremental progress rather than a breakthrough, but in materials science, incremental progress on a hard problem is exactly how you eventually get to usable technology. For the semiconductor industry, finding a viable copper replacement for on-chip and package-level interconnects is a serious long-term concern — copper's resistivity at small dimensions is a known scaling challenge. This research won't change anything in the next product cycle, but it's the kind of foundational work that matters for the decade ahead.
