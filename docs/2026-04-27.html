AI can now cost more than human workers — the economics are shifting
An Axios analysis flagged on Hacker News examines a surprising new dynamic: for certain tasks, deploying AI is now more expensive than using human workers. This sounds counterintuitive given the narrative of AI replacing jobs, but it makes sense when you dig into the numbers. For complex, long-horizon agentic tasks that require many API calls, large context windows, and multiple rounds of iteration, token costs can add up faster than an hourly human rate. The analysis also highlights that human workers bundle capabilities — they remember context across sessions, handle ambiguity without explicit prompting, catch their own mistakes, and adapt to changing requirements — things that AI systems still struggle with and often require expensive compensating architectures. This isn't an argument that AI automation is a bad investment, but it is a useful corrective to the assumption that AI is uniformly cheaper. The real picture is task-dependent: highly repetitive, well-defined, high-volume tasks are absolutely cheaper with AI. Complex, ambiguous, or exception-heavy tasks may not be. For engineering teams making build-vs-buy or automate-vs-hire decisions, this analysis provides a useful framework for thinking about total cost. The cost of AI compute is still dropping rapidly, so this calculus will keep shifting — but for right now, it's worth running the actual numbers rather than assuming automation is always the economical choice.

UK cyber chiefs push passkeys over passwords — here's what that actually means
The UK's National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) issued formal guidance recommending that organizations move from passwords to passkeys, marking a significant institutional endorsement of the WebAuthn/FIDO2 standard. Passkeys replace passwords with cryptographic key pairs: a private key stored on your device (protected by biometrics or a PIN) and a public key stored on the server. There's no shared secret to steal, no database of hashed passwords to crack, and phishing for a passkey is essentially impossible because the key is domain-bound. The BBC coverage makes this accessible to non-technical audiences, which matters because the biggest adoption barrier for passkeys is user understanding, not technical feasibility. Major platforms — Apple, Google, Microsoft — have all shipped passkey support, and the developer tooling is mature enough that implementation is no longer a research project. For engineers working on authentication, this is a useful external forcing function: if you're still building new systems with password authentication as the primary mechanism, you're building on a deprecated paradigm. The main real-world friction points are account recovery (what happens when you lose all your devices) and cross-platform synchronization, both of which are improving but not yet fully solved. The NCSC endorsement is likely to accelerate enterprise procurement decisions that have been stalled by 'wait and see' conservatism around passkeys.

New robotic control software lets robots learn from each other across different hardware
Researchers have developed kinematic intelligence software that allows robots to share learned behaviors even when they have fundamentally different physical hardware — different joints, different actuator configurations, different body geometries. This is a significant problem in robotics: most learned control policies are brittle and hardware-specific, meaning a policy trained on one robot doesn't transfer to a different robot even if they're doing the same task. The new software abstracts away hardware specifics by reasoning about kinematics — the geometry of motion — rather than raw actuator commands. A practical implication is that a robot in a warehouse that learns a new manipulation skill could share that knowledge with robots of a different model elsewhere in the facility. The software also reportedly includes mechanisms for avoiding joint damage by learning the physical limits of each hardware configuration — hence 'avoiding jamming their joints.' For teams building robotics applications, this kind of cross-hardware generalization is a prerequisite for deploying at scale without maintaining separate model training pipelines for every hardware variant. It also connects to the broader trend of foundation models in robotics — Google DeepMind's Gemini Robotics-ER work is heading in a similar direction. The research is at the applied science stage, not yet a shipping product, but the direction is clearly toward more general-purpose robotic intelligence.

University subdomains hijacked to serve porn: a lesson in DNS housekeeping
Security researchers found that hundreds of subdomains from dozens of major universities have been hijacked by scammers and are being used to serve pornographic content and potentially other malicious material. The mechanism is straightforward and embarrassingly preventable: universities set up subdomains for specific projects, services, or temporary sites, then abandon them without removing the DNS records. Scammers discover these dangling DNS entries and register or take over the underlying services they point to, effectively piggybacking on the university's domain reputation and SEO authority. This is sometimes called a 'subdomain takeover' attack and it's been a known vulnerability class for years. The reason it keeps happening is organizational: large universities have decentralized IT, many departments manage their own web presence, and subdomain cleanup is nobody's priority until something embarrassing happens. For any organization managing a complex domain with many subdomains — universities, large enterprises, government agencies — this is a useful reminder to audit your DNS regularly and have a process for retiring records when services are decommissioned. Tools like aquatone, Subjack, and similar subdomain enumeration tools make it trivial for attackers to find these vulnerabilities at scale. The reputational damage from your institution's domain serving malware or pornography is substantial and entirely avoidable.

Artemis II broke the human spaceflight distance record — Fred Haise is fine with it
The Artemis II mission broke Fred Haise's long-standing record for the farthest distance a human has traveled from Earth, a record that had stood since the Apollo 13 mission in 1970. Haise, who is now in his 80s, reacted with characteristic grace: 'It wasn't a big deal. It just coincided with the fact that the Moon was farther away from the Earth.' The record was broken because the Moon happened to be near apogee — its farthest point from Earth — during the Artemis II trajectory. Artemis II is a crewed lunar flyby mission, the first crewed mission to travel to the vicinity of the Moon since Apollo 17 in 1972. The mission is a precursor to Artemis III, which aims to land humans on the Moon's south pole. For context, Haise was on Apollo 13 when the spacecraft swung around the Moon on a free-return trajectory after the oxygen tank explosion — he got farther from Earth than any other human, but never got to land on the Moon. The Artemis program has had significant delays and cost overruns over the years, making this milestone meaningful both technically and symbolically. The human spaceflight community tends to mark these records carefully, and Haise's gracious response to having his record broken is a small but genuinely warm story amid a lot of heavy news.
