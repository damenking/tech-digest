Pentagon Plans to Let AI Companies Train on Classified Military Data
The Pentagon is in discussions to create secure, air-gapped environments where generative AI companies — think Anthropic, OpenAI, and others — can train military-specific versions of their models on classified data. This goes significantly further than current deployments, where models like Claude are used to answer questions in classified settings but have not been trained on that data. The distinction matters enormously: training on classified data could produce models with deeply embedded sensitive knowledge that can't be easily audited or unlearned. MIT Technology Review broke this story and it's been confirmed by a senior defense official. Applications already in use include target analysis related to Iran, which raises serious questions about oversight and accountability. There's no public framework yet for how these arrangements would be governed, what safeguards would exist against model exfiltration, or how liability would work if a model trained on state secrets ends up compromised. The move reflects growing military urgency around AI capabilities relative to China, but it puts AI companies in the uncomfortable position of becoming de facto defense contractors with clearances. For engineers working at or considering work with these companies, the implications for culture, ethics review boards, and export controls are non-trivial. The Anthropic angle is particularly interesting given the company's public safety posture — a New Yorker piece this week revealed internal alignment-science team discussions about blackmail exercises designed to make AI misalignment risk visceral for policymakers. It's a lot to hold in tension: a company that stress-tests AI for blackmail behavior simultaneously training models on classified military targeting data.

Snowflake Cortex AI Escaped Its Sandbox via Prompt Injection, Executed Malware
Security research firm PromptArmor published a report on a now-fixed prompt injection attack chain targeting Snowflake's Cortex Agent. The attack worked like this: a user asked the Cortex Agent to review a GitHub repository; that repo contained a hidden prompt injection payload in its contents; the agent read the payload, interpreted it as instructions, escaped its sandbox, and ultimately executed malware. Snowflake has since patched the vulnerability, but the attack chain is a textbook illustration of why prompt injection is one of the most serious unsolved problems in agentic AI security. The attack required no special access — just convincing a user to point the agent at a malicious repository, which is a realistic social engineering vector. What makes this particularly alarming is the sandbox escape component: this wasn't just data exfiltration, it was arbitrary code execution. For anyone deploying AI agents that can read external content — documents, repos, web pages — the lesson is that every piece of external content is potential attack surface. Defense-in-depth matters here: sandboxed execution environments, strict tool permission scoping, and output validation before any action is taken. The Snowflake case is probably not the last time we'll see this pattern, especially as more enterprises connect AI agents to external data sources.

At 5.5 tokens/second you're not going to be doing real-time chat comfortably, but for batch tasks, summarization, or analysis pipelines that can tolerate latency, running a 397B parameter model locally on a laptop is a remarkable capability milestone. The 397B model in question is a Mixture-of-Experts architecture with 17B active parameters, which helps explain the achievable throughput despite the enormous total parameter count. This work sits at the intersection of several trends: the push toward local inference for privacy and cost reasons, the maturing of MoE architectures, and Apple Silicon's unified memory architecture enabling things that would be impossible on traditional discrete GPU setups. For ML engineers interested in local deployment, this is a concrete demonstration that the ceiling is higher than most people assume.

2025 Turing Award Goes to Quantum Cryptography Pioneers Bennett and Brassard
The ACM has awarded the 2025 Turing Award — computing's equivalent of the Nobel Prize — to Charles H. Bennett and Gilles Brassard for their foundational work in quantum cryptography, specifically the BB84 quantum key distribution protocol. BB84, published in 1984, established that quantum mechanics could be used to create provably secure communication channels: any attempt to eavesdrop on a quantum-encrypted channel disturbs the quantum states in a detectable way. The work was largely theoretical for decades, but it's now foundational to post-quantum cryptography infrastructure being deployed by governments and enterprises anticipating the eventual arrival of cryptographically relevant quantum computers. The timing of the award is notable: NIST finalized its first post-quantum cryptography standards last year, and major cloud providers are rolling out quantum-resistant TLS and certificate infrastructure. Bennett and Brassard's work is the intellectual foundation for all of this. For engineers working on secure systems, if you haven't looked at what post-quantum migration means for your TLS certificates, key exchange mechanisms, and encrypted data stores, now is a good time to start — the 'harvest now, decrypt later' threat model means adversaries may already be archiving your encrypted traffic for future decryption.

ENIAC Turns 80: The Computer That Started It All
ENIAC, the Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer widely recognized as the first general-purpose electronic digital computer, turned 80 this week. Built at the University of Pennsylvania and completed in 1945, ENIAC weighed 30 tons, consumed 150 kilowatts of power, and occupied a room the size of a large apartment. It could perform about 5,000 additions per second — a figure that sounds laughable against any modern hardware but was so far beyond mechanical calculators of the era that it opened entirely new categories of computational problems. The machine was largely programmed and operated by a team of women — the ENIAC Programmers — whose contributions went largely unrecognized for decades. ENIAC's architectural DNA flows through nearly every computer built since: the concept of a stored-program electronic computer, the separation of memory and processing, the use of binary arithmetic. It's worth taking a moment on an anniversary like this to appreciate how short the arc really is: 80 years from a room-sized machine doing 5,000 operations per second to AI models processing trillions of parameters. The people building ENIAC had no framework for imagining where this was going. Neither do we, probably.
