Supreme Court Appears Split on Geofence Warrants — A Case That Could Reshape Digital Privacy
The US Supreme Court heard arguments in Chatrie v. United States, a case about whether police can use 'geofence warrants' — court orders demanding that tech companies like Google hand over data on every device present in a geographic area during a specific time window — to identify criminal suspects. The justices appeared genuinely divided, which means this case could go either way. Geofence warrants are controversial because they're essentially dragnet searches: instead of identifying a suspect first and then seeking their data, police cast a wide net and sift through innocent bystanders' location history to find someone who might be guilty. Google's Sensorvault database, which stores historical location data from Android devices, has been the primary target of these warrants. The constitutional question is whether this kind of bulk location data collection constitutes an unreasonable search under the Fourth Amendment, particularly in light of the Supreme Court's 2018 Carpenter decision, which required warrants for cell-site location records. A ruling allowing geofence warrants would give law enforcement a powerful surveillance tool with minimal judicial oversight. A ruling against them would force police to develop more targeted investigative methods. For tech companies, the outcome determines whether their location databases can be compelled as investigative fishing nets. This is one of the most consequential digital privacy cases the Court has taken in years.

For tech platforms, this represents an ongoing shift from 'we aggregate your content for free and you benefit from the traffic' to 'you pay for the content that makes our platforms valuable.' The broader question is whether mandatory payments actually preserve journalism or just prop up incumbent publishers while starving newer digital outlets. The Australian experiment will be a real-world test of that question over the next few years.
