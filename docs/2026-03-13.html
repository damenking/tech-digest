Iran's 'Handala' hackers behind Stryker wiper attack that shut down its Windows network
A destructive wiper attack has taken down Stryker's entire Microsoft/Windows network environment, and the company has admitted it doesn't know how long restoration will take. The culprit appears to be Handala, an Iranian-linked hacktivist group that has increasingly been used as cover for state-sponsored cyberattacks against US and Israeli targets. Wiper malware is particularly nasty because it's not ransomware — there's no negotiation, no key to buy — the data is simply destroyed. Stryker is a major supplier of lifesaving medical devices including surgical equipment, implants, and emergency room hardware, which makes this attack more alarming than a typical corporate breach. The timing fits a pattern of Iranian retaliation amid ongoing US-Iran military tensions. Handala has been active since at least 2023 and has previously targeted Israeli infrastructure, but this attack represents a significant escalation in targeting a major US medical device manufacturer. The 'hacktivism' framing is widely seen as a fig leaf — analysts generally treat Handala as an instrument of Iranian state cyber operations rather than an independent group. What's unclear is how deep the compromise goes and whether operational technology — the systems that actually control device manufacturing — was affected, which would be a far more serious situation. Healthcare and medical device supply chains are notoriously under-secured compared to their criticality. This should be a wake-up call for every medical device and healthcare infrastructure company that has been slow on security modernization.

A bipartisan bill has been introduced that would force the FBI to obtain a warrant before reading Americans' private messages, and would ban federal agencies from purchasing commercial data on US residents as a backdoor to surveillance. The legislation arrives ahead of a critical April deadline related to Section 702 of FISA, which authorizes the government to collect communications of foreign targets but has repeatedly been used to query data about Americans without warrants. The 'commercial data purchase' loophole is particularly significant — intelligence and law enforcement agencies have been buying location data, browsing histories, and other sensitive information from data brokers, effectively bypassing Fourth Amendment protections. The bipartisan nature of this bill is notable given how rarely surveillance reform gets cross-aisle support. Privacy advocates have been pushing for this reform for years, but national security hawks have consistently blocked or watered down such efforts. The bill's chances depend heavily on whether the current administration is willing to accept limits on FBI surveillance tools. For engineers building apps and services that handle user data, this is a relevant development — a successful bill would significantly change what government agencies can demand or simply buy. Even if this specific bill fails, the fact that it has bipartisan backing signals growing political appetite for surveillance reform. The commercial data purchase ban would affect a multi-billion dollar data broker industry that has largely operated in a legal gray zone.

OpenAI's research on designing AI agents to resist prompt injection
OpenAI has published research detailing how ChatGPT's agentic workflows are being engineered to defend against prompt injection and social engineering attacks. The core approach involves constraining what actions agents can take based on context, limiting access to sensitive data in agent workflows, and building hierarchical instruction systems where trusted instructions take precedence over potentially malicious injected content. Prompt injection is one of the most serious practical security threats to AI agents — it's essentially a way to hijack an AI system by embedding adversarial instructions in content the agent reads or processes. As AI agents are given more capabilities (browsing the web, executing code, reading files, sending emails), the consequences of a successful injection attack grow from 'embarrassing' to 'catastrophic'. The companion paper on 'instruction hierarchy' (IH-Challenge) describes a training methodology that teaches models to prioritize instructions from trusted sources over instructions from untrusted ones — essentially giving models a sense of provenance for the commands they receive. This is hard because LLMs by design are trained to be helpful and follow instructions, which makes them naturally susceptible to convincing-sounding injected commands. The research is noteworthy because it's addressing a real, practical, currently-being-exploited vulnerability rather than speculative future AI risks. Combined with the Promptfoo acquisition, there's a coherent story here about OpenAI building serious security infrastructure for the agentic era. Anyone building production AI agents should read both papers.

Document poisoning in RAG systems: a real and underappreciated attack vector
A technical writeup on document poisoning attacks against RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) systems is making the rounds among developers building production AI applications. The attack involves embedding adversarial content into documents that an AI system retrieves and uses as context — essentially corrupting the AI's knowledge sources so it produces incorrect, misleading, or harmful outputs. RAG architectures are extremely common in enterprise AI deployments because they allow AI systems to work with proprietary or current data without retraining; this attack vector targets the documents in that retrieval corpus. Unlike prompt injection (which targets the model directly), document poisoning is harder to detect because the malicious content lives in what looks like a normal document in your knowledge base. The attack is relevant for any system that ingests external documents — customer support bots trained on help articles, code assistants that read documentation, research tools that index papers. The writeup presumably covers detection and mitigation strategies, which is the practically useful part. This connects directly to the OpenAI security work on prompt injection resistance and the Promptfoo acquisition — there's a whole emerging field of 'AI application security' that the industry is only beginning to take seriously. If you're building RAG systems, this is required reading.
