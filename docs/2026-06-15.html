This is an extraordinary intervention — the US government effectively reaching into a private company's operations and telling them which people can use their own software. Simon Willison called it 'nuts,' and it's hard to disagree — we've moved from export controls on chips to export controls on AI model access itself. Google DeepMind invests $10M in multi-agent AI safety research amid growing concerns about agent interactions
Google DeepMind has announced a $10 million funding call for research into multi-agent AI safety — specifically the risks that emerge when millions of AI agents begin interacting with each other at scale. Rohin Shah, who directs DeepMind's AGI safety and alignment research, has flagged that the mass-market arrival of agents capable of acting without human oversight and of following instructions from other agents creates qualitatively new risks that current safety research doesn't adequately address. The concern isn't just about any single agent behaving badly — it's about emergent, systemic behaviors that arise from interactions among vast numbers of agents, none of which individually appears problematic. Think of it like financial markets: individual traders following reasonable rules can collectively produce flash crashes and cascading failures. At the scale of millions of AI agents embedded in enterprise and consumer workflows, the potential for unexpected systemic effects is real and poorly understood. This is one of the more intellectually honest safety investments we've seen from a major lab — it's not about marketing, it's about a specific technical problem that researchers are genuinely worried about. The $10M is a relatively modest sum for a problem of this scope, which either means DeepMind sees this as seed funding for a larger program or that the research community capable of working on this problem is still quite small. For developers building agentic systems, this research agenda is worth watching because the findings could shape how future agentic frameworks are designed.

Google DeepMind releases DiffusionGemma with 4x faster text generation
Google DeepMind has released DiffusionGemma, a new model architecture that achieves text generation speeds roughly four times faster than comparable autoregressive approaches. Diffusion-based language models have been a research topic for a few years, but getting them to actually outperform transformer-based autoregressive models on practical text generation tasks has proven genuinely difficult. If DiffusionGemma delivers on the 4x speed claim in real-world conditions, this could be a meaningful inflection point for inference efficiency — especially relevant for applications where latency matters, like interactive agents and real-time coding assistants. The architectural shift away from autoregressive token-by-token generation is significant because it changes how models can be deployed and optimized at the infrastructure level. It's also notable that DeepMind is building this into the Gemma open-weights lineage, which means developers will be able to experiment with and build on this approach directly. Speed improvements at this magnitude tend to have compounding effects: cheaper inference means more API calls become economically viable, which expands the space of applications that make sense to build. The release comes alongside Gemma 4 12B, a new unified encoder-free multimodal model, suggesting DeepMind is shipping at a high cadence right now. Worth keeping an eye on independent benchmarks to verify the 4x claim before drawing strong conclusions.

Why AI hasn't replaced software engineers, and won't — a counterargument worth reading

Their argument is particularly worth taking seriously because they're examining the profession uniquely positioned to be disrupted first — if AI were going to hollow out any knowledge work category quickly, software engineering would be the obvious candidate. Their core claim is that the evidence doesn't support the threshold narrative: productivity gains from AI coding tools have been real but haven't translated into reduced headcount, because the demand for software has expanded to absorb the productivity gains. This is basically the same dynamic that played out with spreadsheets and database tools — they made individual developers more productive without eliminating the profession. There's a steelman version of the 'AI will replace engineers' argument that Narayanan and Kapoor engage with seriously, which makes the essay more intellectually honest than most takes in either direction. The piece is timely given the AI layoff discussions happening simultaneously — it's useful to distinguish between AI-driven layoffs in specific companies or roles versus a structural collapse of software engineering as a profession. For working engineers this is reassuring, but the caveat worth holding onto is that 'the profession survives' doesn't mean every individual's current role survives. Highly recommended reading for anyone anxious about the medium-term trajectory of their career.
