JWST maps weather on a hot gas giant 700 light-years away — and finds something unexpected
The James Webb Space Telescope has produced the first detailed weather maps of a hot Jupiter exoplanet approximately 700 light-years away, and the results are revealing unexpected asymmetries that could affect how scientists study planetary atmospheres more broadly. Hot Jupiters are gas giants that orbit extremely close to their stars, tidally locked so one side always faces the star and the other faces away — creating extreme temperature gradients. JWST's infrared sensitivity allows it to detect the thermal emission from different parts of the planet's atmosphere as it orbits, effectively building a temperature map. The unexpected finding is that the atmospheric differences between the day and night sides — and potentially between the leading and trailing hemispheres — don't match existing models cleanly. This matters because those models are used as a baseline for studying all planetary atmospheres, including potentially habitable worlds. If the models are off for hot Jupiters, researchers need to understand why before confidently applying similar approaches to smaller, cooler planets where habitability is actually in question. For anyone who followed the early Webb results with excitement, this is the kind of second-order science that makes the telescope's long-term value clear: it's not just taking pretty pictures, it's stress-testing our theoretical frameworks.

Cleve Moler, creator of MATLAB, has died
Cleve Moler, the mathematician and computer scientist who created MATLAB, has passed away. This is a significant loss for the scientific and engineering computing community. MATLAB was originally written by Moler in the late 1970s as a way to give students easy access to LINPACK and EISPACK linear algebra routines without needing to write Fortran, and it became one of the most widely used scientific computing environments in the world. For a generation — arguably several generations — of engineers, scientists, and researchers, MATLAB was the primary tool for numerical computation, signal processing, control systems, and data analysis. Moler co-founded MathWorks in 1984 to commercialize MATLAB, and the company remains independent and employee-owned, which is genuinely unusual for a software company of its scale and influence. The scientific Python ecosystem (NumPy, SciPy, matplotlib) drew direct inspiration from MATLAB's design, meaning Moler's influence extends well beyond users who ever touched a MATLAB license. He was also known for being genuinely engaged with the user community well into his later career — writing, teaching, and contributing to the numerical computing conversation. The Hacker News thread (score 63) reflects genuine respect and nostalgia from engineers across many fields whose work was built on tools he created.
