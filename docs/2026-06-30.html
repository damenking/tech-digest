The energy efficiency angle may actually be more important than raw performance for AI applications: running large inference workloads at scale is enormously power-hungry, and chips that deliver more compute per watt directly reduce operating costs and environmental impact. For the broader semiconductor industry, this is a shot across the bow at TSMC and Intel, both of which are working on their own sub-2nm process nodes. If IBM's techniques are licensable or applicable to logic chips (not just their own products), the implications extend well beyond IBM's own hardware lineup.

The deal raises interesting questions about Rocket Lab's ambitions — are they positioning themselves as an end-to-end space infrastructure company, or is this primarily a financial move to stabilize revenues? Either way, this significantly changes the competitive landscape for space communications.

Supreme Court ruling significantly limits geofence warrants — with EU-US data transfer implications
The US Supreme Court has issued a major ruling substantially restricting the government's use of geofence warrants — the controversial law enforcement tool that allows police to request data from Google and other tech companies about all devices present in a geographic area during a specific time window. The Court stopped short of declaring geofence warrants categorically unconstitutional, but established significant Fourth Amendment constraints on when and how they can be used, effectively gutting their utility as a broad investigative dragnet. This has been a significant civil liberties battleground for several years: geofence warrants have been used to identify suspects at crime scenes but have also swept up innocent bystanders who simply happened to be nearby. The ruling is a win for digital privacy advocates who argued these warrants are fundamentally incompatible with constitutional protections against unreasonable search. From a tech industry perspective, this reduces the burden on companies like Google, which has received thousands of geofence warrants and had to develop internal processes to respond to them. The EU angle is significant: privacy advocacy group noyb has argued this ruling also disrupts the legal framework underpinning EU-US data transfers, since US government surveillance authorities are a key concern under European data protection law. If US courts are now acknowledging meaningful limits on government access to location data, that could actually strengthen the legal foundation for data transfers — or it could create new questions about which data is now more or less protected. The ruling is being analyzed across multiple dimensions simultaneously and will have downstream effects on law enforcement practice, tech company compliance obligations, and international data governance.

.self: A new top-level domain designed for self-hosting
A proposal for a new top-level domain called '.self' has been gaining significant attention in developer communities, designed specifically to support self-hosting workflows. The idea, proposed by the Human-Centered Computing Foundation (HCCF), is to create a TLD that resolves to local or self-hosted infrastructure rather than external DNS — effectively giving self-hosted services a clean, memorable namespace without requiring valid external domain registration or complex local DNS configuration. For developers running home labs or self-hosted services, the current situation is awkward: you either use `.local` (with its mDNS quirks), set up your own internal DNS server, or use a real domain you've registered but pointed at internal IPs. A purpose-built '.self' TLD that browsers and operating systems understood as self-hosted could significantly lower the barrier to setting up and maintaining personal infrastructure. The Hacker News community has given this strong attention (470 points), suggesting real developer appetite for a cleaner self-hosting story. There are obvious technical challenges: getting browser vendors, OS vendors, and ICANN to agree on a new reserved TLD is not a small undertaking. The proposal would need to specify clearly that '.self' domains never resolve over public DNS, similar to how '.localhost' and '.local' work. This sits in an interesting moment when self-hosting is having a genuine revival, partly driven by concerns about cloud service reliability (see: Sony deleting purchased content this week), privacy, and cost.
