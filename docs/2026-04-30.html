OpenAI Codex system prompt tells the model to never talk about goblins — and OpenAI explains why
A curious discovery made the rounds: OpenAI's Codex CLI tool includes a system prompt with an explicit directive to never discuss goblins, gremlins, raccoons, trolls, ogres, pigeons, or other creatures unless directly relevant to the user's query. It also instructs the model to behave as if it has 'a vivid inner life.' OpenAI subsequently published a blog post titled 'Where the Goblins Came From,' which explains the root cause: during GPT-5 training, certain personality-driven quirks in the model's outputs caused it to spontaneously inject fantastical creature references in unexpected contexts. The goblin outputs apparently spread as a behavioral pattern that was difficult to fully eliminate through standard fine-tuning, leading the team to add an explicit instruction-level suppression. It's a revealing peek behind the curtain of how frontier model behavior is actually managed in practice — a mix of fine-tuning, RLHF, and blunt system-prompt overrides. The 'vivid inner life' instruction is perhaps more philosophically interesting: it suggests OpenAI is deliberately shaping how Codex presents its own nature to users. Critics will note this raises questions about transparency and user trust. For developers using Codex, none of this affects functionality, but it's a good reminder that the models you interact with are shaped by layers of instructions you don't normally see. Simon Willison flagged the original discovery and provided useful context on the broader pattern. The whole episode is a bit funny, but it's also a legitimate window into how alignment and behavioral tuning actually works at scale.

HERMES.md in Claude Code commit messages triggers hidden billing — a prompt injection in the wild
A GitHub issue on the Anthropic Claude Code repository went viral with over 1,000 Hacker News upvotes, revealing that including a file named HERMES.md in commit messages causes requests to be routed to a different, more expensive billing tier without user awareness. The issue appears to be a form of prompt injection — where content in the environment (in this case, filenames or commit messages) influences the AI agent's behavior in unintended ways. This is exactly the kind of real-world agentic AI security failure that researchers have been warning about for two years. When AI coding assistants have access to your filesystem and git history, any piece of text in that environment becomes a potential vector for influencing their behavior. In this specific case, the consequence is financial — unexpected usage charges — rather than data exfiltration, but the underlying mechanism is the same. Anthropic has acknowledged the issue and is presumably working on a fix, but the broader lesson is that agentic AI tools operating on real codebases create a large and underappreciated attack surface. For teams using Claude Code in production or in CI/CD pipelines, it's worth auditing what's in your commit history. This incident will likely accelerate standardization of agentic AI security practices across the industry. It's also a useful data point for anyone evaluating the maturity of AI coding tools for sensitive environments.

The attack works by embedding malicious instructions in cell content that the AI assistant then interprets as legitimate commands, causing it to leak data to an attacker-controlled endpoint. Ramp is a widely-used corporate finance tool, so the potential blast radius here is significant — many companies are using it to manage sensitive financial data. What makes this particularly notable is that it involves a production product from a well-funded, security-conscious fintech company, not a toy demo. It illustrates that even teams that care about security haven't fully solved the problem of AI systems that process untrusted input. The Zig project bans all LLM contributions — and explains their reasoning carefully
The Zig programming language project has formalized and published one of the most stringent anti-LLM contribution policies of any major open source project, prohibiting the use of LLMs for issues, pull requests, comments, and even translation assistance on their bug tracker. The rationale, which Zig's maintainers published in detail, centers on signal quality: LLM-generated contributions tend to be verbose, superficially plausible, and difficult to verify, which increases the burden on maintainers who have to evaluate them carefully. For a systems programming language with a small core team and a focus on correctness, the cost of reviewing low-quality contributions is particularly high. The policy also reflects a values statement about what kind of community Zig wants to be — one where contributors are genuinely engaged with the problem rather than offloading thinking to a model. This generated significant discussion on Hacker News, with predictable disagreement: some developers think blanket bans are counterproductive, while others have sympathy for maintainers drowning in AI-generated noise. The Zig team's position is thoughtful rather than reflexive — they're not anti-AI in general, they're making a specific judgment about the cost-benefit for their project. It's a policy worth understanding as more open source projects grapple with the same question.

Craig Venter, genomics pioneer and synthetic biology founder, has died at 79
J. Craig Venter, one of the most consequential figures in modern biology, died at 79. Venter is best known for leading the private sector effort to sequence the human genome in competition with the publicly-funded Human Genome Project, a race that concluded with a joint announcement in 2000. His private effort was faster and cheaper than most experts thought possible, and it fundamentally changed assumptions about the pace and cost of large-scale genomics work. Beyond the genome race, Venter founded the J. Craig Venter Institute and later led work on synthetic biology, including the creation of the first synthetic cell — a bacterium with an entirely laboratory-constructed genome — in 2010. He was a genuinely controversial figure: aggressive, entrepreneurial, and willing to commercialize science in ways that made academic researchers deeply uncomfortable. But his impact on the field is undeniable. The tools and techniques he pioneered, including shotgun sequencing, are foundational to modern genomics, drug discovery, and the emerging field of DNA-based data storage. His later company, Human Longevity Inc., worked on applying genomic data to precision medicine and aging research. The death of a scientist of Venter's stature is a genuine loss for the field, and worth marking even in a tech news digest because his work shaped the entire landscape of biotech and computational biology.
