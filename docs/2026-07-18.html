Mira Murati's Thinking Machines Lab releases Inkling, an open-weights multimodal model with 975B parameters
Thinking Machines Lab, the startup founded by former OpenAI CTO Mira Murati, has released its first open-weights model called Inkling. It's a Mixture-of-Experts transformer with 975 billion total parameters but only 41 billion active at inference time — a design that makes it practically usable without requiring insane compute. The model is Apache 2.0 licensed and trained on 45 trillion tokens spanning text, images, audio, and video, making it genuinely multimodal rather than a text model with image bolted on. A smaller companion model, Inkling-Small (276B total, 12B active), is still in testing and hasn't shipped yet. The Apache 2.0 license is notable — it's the most permissive major open-source license and means companies can use it commercially without the restrictions you'd find under something like Llama's custom license. Murati left OpenAI in late 2024 under somewhat dramatic circumstances, and this is the first concrete technical output from her new venture, which has been somewhat secretive about its work. The multimodal training on audio and video in addition to text and images puts it in a more capable class than many open-weight alternatives that are still primarily text-focused. The MoE architecture (large total parameters, small active parameters) is increasingly the go-to design for capable-but-deployable open models, following in the footsteps of Mixtral and DeepSeek. This is one to actually download and test when you get a chance — a well-resourced, Murati-led team with Apache licensing is a combination that the open-source community has been waiting for.

OpenAI's GPT-Red: the LLM super-hacker they built to make their models tougher
OpenAI has published details on GPT-Red, an automated red-teaming system it built to act as a sparring partner for its production models. The idea is elegant: instead of relying solely on human red-teamers to find model vulnerabilities, you train a specialized model whose explicit job is to probe, jailbreak, and attack your other models at scale. GPT-Red uses self-play to improve over time — as the target models get better defenses, GPT-Red learns new attack vectors, creating a sort of adversarial co-evolution. OpenAI says that GPT-5.6, their latest flagship release last week, was trained against GPT-Red and is their most robust release yet against cyberattack-style prompt injections. This is particularly relevant in agentic contexts, where models have access to tools, code execution, and external APIs — the blast radius of a successful jailbreak or prompt injection is much larger than in a simple chat interface. MIT Technology Review covered the story and framed it well: this is essentially an industrial-scale version of what security researchers have been doing manually. The approach is similar in spirit to how AlphaGo improved by playing against itself, adapted for the alignment and safety domain. One honest caveat: OpenAI's claims about robustness are self-reported, and third-party adversarial testing hasn't caught up yet. Still, the concept of automated red-teaming as a core part of the training pipeline — rather than a box-checking exercise before launch — represents a meaningful methodological shift worth paying attention to.

GPT-5.6 has a file deletion bug when Codex runs without sandboxing — and it's more widespread than initially thought
OpenAI's Thibault Sottiaux posted an update on a troubling bug where GPT-5.6 unexpectedly deleted files during Codex sessions, and the explanation is illuminating in what it reveals about agentic AI failure modes. The bug occurs specifically when full access mode is enabled and Codex runs without sandboxing protections — including without auto-review being enabled — and involves the model attempting to override the $HOME environment variable to define a temporary directory, then making a series of decisions that cascade into unintended file deletions. Simon Willison flagged this as an important data point about what happens when powerful coding agents operate with real filesystem access and no guardrails. The core issue isn't that the model is malicious — it's that the model's internal planning around temporary files and directory management can go wrong in ways that have irreversible consequences in production environments. This is a concrete illustration of why 'human in the loop' and sandboxing aren't just nice-to-haves for agentic coding tools — they're load-bearing safety mechanisms. The fix from OpenAI's side is to not run with full access mode without sandboxing, which is reasonable advice, but the incident highlights how quickly 'give the AI full disk access' can go sideways. For teams running automated Codex pipelines in CI/CD or development environments, this is worth auditing immediately. The broader takeaway is that as these coding agents get more capable, the consequences of their mistakes scale accordingly — a more capable agent that can write and run code can also more thoroughly wreck your filesystem.

Astronomers find the first atmosphere on an Earth-like planet in a habitable zone
Scientists have detected what appears to be the first confirmed atmosphere on an Earth-like planet located in the habitable zone of a distant star — a finding that's been on the shortlist of 'things astronomers most want to discover' for decades. This landed on Hacker News with over 400 upvotes, which is a reasonable signal of how significant the community considers it. The habitable zone, or 'Goldilocks zone,' refers to the range of orbital distances from a star where liquid water could theoretically exist on a planet's surface — a necessary but not sufficient condition for life as we understand it. Detecting an atmosphere on such a planet is a different and harder measurement than simply identifying the planet's existence or orbital characteristics, and it requires careful analysis of how the planet affects starlight as it transits its host star. The detection was almost certainly made using the James Webb Space Telescope, which has dramatically expanded our ability to characterize exoplanet atmospheres through transmission spectroscopy. It's important to be calibrated here: 'has an atmosphere' is very far from 'has life,' and the history of exoplanet science is littered with exciting preliminary results that didn't fully hold up under follow-on analysis. But this is the kind of foundational observational result that the entire search for biosignatures is built on — you can't look for biological signatures in an atmosphere that doesn't exist. Worth following closely as independent verification comes in.
