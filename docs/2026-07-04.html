LLMs are stuck in a groupthink groove — and a startup is trying to fix it
MIT Technology Review highlights a well-documented but underappreciated flaw in large language models: they exhibit a kind of statistical groupthink, consistently converging on the same outputs even when asked for variety or randomness. The classic example is asking a chatbot for a random number between 1 and 10 — you'll almost always get 7, then 3 or 4, then 8 or 9. This isn't a bug so much as a structural property of how LLMs are trained: they learn to predict the most likely next token based on patterns in training data, which means they inherit the distribution of human responses, not true randomness or genuine diversity of thought. The implications go beyond party tricks — it matters for brainstorming, scientific hypothesis generation, decision support, and any use case where you actually want diverse perspectives or exploratory thinking. A startup covered in this piece is working on mechanisms to force more genuine diversity in model outputs, though the article is light on technical specifics. Approaches in this space range from temperature tuning and diverse sampling strategies to training-time interventions that reward output diversity. The challenge is that you can't just add noise — you want diverse but still coherent, useful outputs. This is a genuinely hard problem that sits at the intersection of probability theory, cognitive science, and ML training dynamics. It's worth keeping an eye on, especially for teams building applications where AI is supposed to help explore a solution space rather than just retrieve the most probable answer.

AI's volatile power draw is quietly stressing the electrical grid
IEEE Spectrum has a detailed piece on a problem that's getting less attention than raw data center energy consumption: the volatility of AI workloads is causing grid instability that's harder to manage than steady-state power demand. AI inference workloads in particular are extremely spiky — a data center can swing between near-idle and full load in seconds depending on request volume, which is a fundamentally different challenge from, say, running a factory or even a crypto mining farm. Grid operators are built around the assumption that large industrial loads ramp up and down on predictable schedules; AI data centers don't follow those patterns. This volatility can cause frequency deviations, voltage instability, and force grid operators to maintain expensive spinning reserves they wouldn't otherwise need. The problem scales with AI adoption — as more workloads move to inference at scale, more of the grid's load profile becomes unpredictable. Solutions being explored include on-site battery storage to buffer demand spikes, smarter load scheduling by cloud providers, and demand response agreements with utilities. But these are engineering band-aids on what may be a structural mismatch between how AI infrastructure is operated and how power grids are designed to handle large customers. This is an underreported story with real infrastructure consequences, and it deserves attention from anyone thinking about the long-term sustainability of AI at scale.

FAA proposes allowing quiet supersonic flights over US cities
The FAA has put forward a regulatory proposal that would allow supersonic aircraft to fly over US cities, provided they meet noise standards stringent enough to eliminate the sonic boom problem. This is a significant policy shift. The current ban on supersonic flight over US land has been in place since the 1970s, largely killing the commercial supersonic market in America — it's why the Concorde never flew domestic US routes. Several startups, most notably Boom Supersonic, have been developing supersonic aircraft with the expectation that regulations would eventually catch up to the technology. The FAA's proposal doesn't simply lift the ban; it creates a performance standard — you can fly supersonic if you can demonstrate your aircraft is quiet enough. This is smart regulation in principle: it's technology-neutral and gives manufacturers a clear target to design toward. The practical question is whether any aircraft can actually meet the noise standard at supersonic speeds, since the sonic boom is a physical consequence of exceeding the speed of sound, though 'low boom' designs using shaped fuselages can reduce — if not eliminate — the shockwave signature at ground level. If this rule is finalized, it could accelerate the timeline for supersonic commercial aviation in the US, with implications for business travel and eventually consumer aviation.

Device that revives donor eyeballs could make whole-eye transplants possible
MIT Technology Review covers a promising development in transplant medicine: a device designed to maintain and revitalize human eyes after removal from deceased donors, potentially enabling whole-eye transplants that could restore sight. This is technically very hard. Eyes begin degenerating almost immediately after death, and the optic nerve — which carries visual signals from the retina to the brain — does not regenerate in humans the way peripheral nerves can. A previous attempt at a whole-eye transplant resulted in an eye that was structurally integrated but unable to see, largely because the optic nerve connection wasn't re-established. The new device apparently addresses the preservation problem by maintaining the eye in a more viable state for longer, giving surgeons more time to work and potentially enabling better surgical outcomes. Restoring the optic nerve connection remains the core scientific challenge, and researchers are investigating whether techniques used in other nerve regeneration contexts could be adapted. The potential patient population includes people who lost eyes to injury or disease where the visual cortex and optic pathway are intact — restoring sight in these cases would be transformative. This is early-stage research, but it's a credible step toward something that would have seemed like science fiction a decade ago.

AI-driven chip shortage is pushing consumer electronics prices higher — again
Wired reports that consumer electronics prices are rising again across phones, computers, and gaming consoles, this time driven by an AI-induced chip shortage rather than the pandemic supply chain disruptions that characterized 2021-2022. The mechanism is straightforward: the enormous demand for AI accelerators — primarily Nvidia GPUs and custom AI chips — is consuming semiconductor fab capacity and driving up the cost of advanced process nodes, which in turn raises costs for chips used in consumer devices. TSMC and Samsung's leading-edge fabs are heavily allocated to AI chip production, leaving less capacity for the display drivers, application processors, and other chips that go into phones and laptops. Unlike the pandemic shortage, which was a supply shock, this is a demand-driven shortage with a self-reinforcing quality: as AI applications become more capable and valuable, demand for AI chips grows, further crowding out consumer silicon. The practical implication for consumers is that the upgrade cycle assumptions that made sense two years ago no longer apply — prices aren't falling the way they used to, and the expectation that next year's model will be meaningfully cheaper than this year's is less reliable. For enterprise buyers, this affects hardware refresh planning in meaningful ways. It's also worth noting that this dynamic creates a perverse incentive structure where consumer hardware quality improvements slow down because margins are being squeezed.
