Cerebras Files for IPO With AWS Deal and $10B+ OpenAI Agreement in Hand

The competitive landscape remains fierce: Nvidia's dominance is enormous, and AMD, Intel, and a dozen other chip startups are all competing for the same alternative-to-Nvidia dollars. Whether Cerebras's wafer-scale approach can scale commercially across a broad range of workloads, or whether it's a specialized solution for specific inference tasks, is the key question for investors. For engineers, the more interesting story is whether Cerebras's software toolchain — historically a pain point for non-Nvidia hardware — has matured enough to make adoption practical.

Sam Altman's World Expands Human Verification to Tinder and Zoom
World — previously known as Worldcoin — Sam Altman's iris-scanning identity verification project, is expanding its 'proof of humanity' infrastructure to Tinder and Zoom, among other new partners. The idea is straightforward in principle: you look into a physical Orb device, it scans your iris, and you get a cryptographic credential proving you're a real human. Tinder wants this to combat fake profiles and bot accounts; Zoom presumably wants it for similar reasons around meeting authenticity and identity verification. This is the project's most mainstream consumer-facing deployment yet, moving beyond its crypto-adjacent origins into everyday applications that hundreds of millions of people actually use. The expansion is genuinely interesting from a technical and policy standpoint: biometric identity at scale has real benefits for combating AI-generated fake accounts, but also real risks around centralization of biometric data and what happens to that data under various legal jurisdictions. Altman's pitch is that the Orb system is privacy-preserving — it stores a hash of your iris pattern rather than the image itself — but trust in that claim requires trusting World's infrastructure and governance, which is not the same as cryptographic verification. For Tinder specifically, the bot and fake profile problem is genuinely severe, and iris scanning would be a meaningful deterrent — it's much harder to manufacture biometric credentials at scale than to spin up fake email accounts. Zoom's use case is less obvious but points toward a world where video meeting participants can cryptographically attest they are who they claim to be — useful for high-stakes business and government contexts. The deeper question is whether World's Orb network can scale globally without becoming a surveillance infrastructure that various governments will want access to. The partnership with Tinder in particular brings this into sharp relief: it's one thing to voluntarily iris-scan for a crypto wallet, another when it becomes a practical requirement to use a major dating platform.

For the broader AI industry, construction delays mean that the compute capacity being promised in announcements and investor presentations may not materialize on the timelines assumed. This has downstream implications for model training schedules, cloud provider capacity, and the competitive dynamics between hyperscalers. The energy constraint in particular is not a problem that money alone can solve quickly — grid expansion and generation capacity operate on decade-scale timelines. Anyone building business plans around specific AI compute availability windows should be stress-testing those assumptions against realistic construction and grid connection timelines.

This fits a broader pattern of consumer device makers progressively tightening platform control under the rationale of security and user experience, while critics argue it's primarily about revenue and lock-in. The lack of a clear regulatory framework requiring open sideloading on streaming devices — unlike the ongoing debates about smartphone ecosystems — means Amazon faces little external pressure on this decision. For developers who have built apps distributed primarily through sideloading on Fire TV devices, this is a concrete problem that requires either getting into Amazon's store or accepting a shrinking install base.

NIST Scientists Create 'Any Wavelength' Lasers on Tiny Circuits
Scientists at NIST have developed a technique for creating lasers at essentially any wavelength on small integrated circuits, a breakthrough that could have significant implications for optical communications, sensing, and photonic computing. The ability to generate arbitrary wavelengths on-chip has been a long-standing challenge in photonics — historically, laser wavelengths are determined by the physical properties of the gain medium, limiting designers to specific wavelengths unless they used bulky and expensive external components. NIST's approach apparently overcomes this constraint in a way that's compatible with small-scale integrated fabrication, which is the key enabler for practical deployment. For optical communications, arbitrary wavelength generation on-chip could enable denser wavelength-division multiplexing — fitting more channels of data onto a single fiber — or simplify the design of transceivers for data center interconnects. The photonic computing angle is potentially more exciting in the long run: photonic logic gates and interconnects could dramatically reduce the energy consumption of compute-intensive operations, which is an increasingly urgent problem as AI workloads drive data center power consumption to new heights. The story from IEEE Spectrum on 'squishy photonic switches' published this same week is adjacent: soft photonic switches using liquid droplets could enable a different approach to fast, low-power photonic logic. Together, these stories suggest that photonics is having a productive research moment, with multiple teams exploring different pathways toward practical optical computing. The gap between NIST lab demonstrations and commercial products is substantial — fabrication at scale, reliability, and integration with existing silicon processes all need to be solved — but the directional progress is real.

Stealth Signals Are Bypassing Iran's Internet Blackout
IEEE Spectrum has a detailed piece on how people inside Iran are circumventing the near-total communications shutdown that the Iranian government imposed on January 8, 2026 — the country's first full information blackout, covering all provinces simultaneously. The technical methods are varied and adaptive: satellite TV broadcast signals are being repurposed to carry internet data, exploiting the fact that it's much easier for a government to block bidirectional internet connections than broadcast signals received by millions of existing satellite dishes. This is a clever adaptation — satellite dishes are ubiquitous in Iran despite being technically illegal, and the broadcast spectrum is harder to shut down without disrupting state television, which the government obviously wants to keep running. Other techniques involve ham radio operators, sneakernet data transfer, and exploiting timing windows when the blackout is partially lifted for economic or operational reasons. The piece is worth reading in full for the technical creativity involved, but the broader story is about the arms race between state censorship infrastructure and the ingenuity of people who need to communicate. Iran's blackout is more comprehensive than previous attempts — which were typically limited to specific platforms or time windows — and represents a more sophisticated effort at full information control. The techniques being used to bypass it also represent an evolution of the circumvention methods documented in earlier crackdowns. For security and network engineers, the case study is useful: it illustrates which parts of the communications stack are hardest for a state actor to shut down completely, and which are vulnerable. Satellite broadcast in particular emerges as a resilient channel precisely because of its asymmetric, one-to-many architecture.

NASA Shuts Off Voyager 1 Instrument to Keep the Spacecraft Alive
NASA has shut down one of Voyager 1's science instruments to preserve enough power to keep the rest of the spacecraft operating — a poignant but necessary decision as the 48-year-old probe continues its journey through interstellar space. Voyager 1 is the most distant human-made object ever built, currently more than 24 billion kilometers from Earth, and its continued operation is a testament to the extraordinary engineering and mission planning of the 1970s team that designed it. Power from the spacecraft's radioisotope thermoelectric generators has been declining at roughly 4 watts per year, and the mission team has been making increasingly difficult tradeoffs about which instruments to keep running and which to sacrifice. Each shutdown is irreversible — once an instrument is powered down in the cold of interstellar space, it cannot be restarted — which makes these decisions genuinely weighty. The light-travel delay of roughly 22.5 hours each way means commanding the spacecraft requires planning on timescales that most software engineers would find disorienting. Voyager 1 is still returning science data: it's the only spacecraft measuring the interstellar medium directly, providing unique data about the boundary where the Sun's influence ends and true interstellar space begins. The mission is expected to be able to operate at least some instruments into the early 2030s before power becomes insufficient to maintain any science. It's worth pausing to appreciate that humanity put an object on a trajectory that will take it to the vicinity of another star — in about 40,000 years — and we're still talking to it.
